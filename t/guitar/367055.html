PaulT August 1, 2016, 12:45am #6

If you want to play with other people or improvise, practice playing scales in rhythm and playing with the radio or CDs. It’s good to know some blues licks, too, make the guitar sing. The pentatonic scale, the blues scale, the gypsy jazz scale, etc… are all good to use. Learn about the circle of fifths and the relative minor seventh. Learn the E major shape barre chord and the A major shape barre chord, which can be moved around and easily modified into minor suspended seventh or ninth chords. Don’t play Christian music, it makes you look weak and stupid.

Unhandled August 1, 2016, 12:45am #10

You need a deeper understanding of music theory to improve beyond what you’re doing now.

Like PaulT said, learn your scales. Start with ionian (major) and work your way through the modes, learn pentatonic, and minor pentatonic (blues scale). The nice thing about guitar is once you learn it in one key you know it in all keys. Learn how chords are constructed based on the degrees of these scales. Work on reading music. Learn about key signatures (goes hand in hand with cycle of 5ths/4ths), different rhythms, the concept of comping and swing time.

Christian rock is generally pretty basic so you’re going to have to branch out a bit if you really want to be a well rounded guitarist.

Unhandled August 1, 2016, 12:45am #20
Mopman:

So you DID just mean learning the fretboard. You’re right then, I guess, but I don’t see it as a big enough part of learning an instrument to be a big deal

I can learn one scale on guitar and instantly be able to play it in every key. To do the same on trumpet would essentially require me to learn 11 other entirely different fingering patterns.

Just for comparison, most competent musicians would know at the very least the 7 modern modes plus say 3 more scales. A guitarist can memorize just those 10 patterns and be done with it, something easily accomplished in a week. For a trumpet player, they must learn 120 different patterns to be at the same level as the guitarist.

Will a trumpet player often be using an F# locrian scale? Probably not, but if you’re improvising and the chord construction happens to match it, you had better know it or be able to think of it in your head pretty damn fast.
