Tile based is basically using 2D physics with AABBs. You won’t be able to do a lot of interesting things if you really want to get into physics. Just keeping in 2D is fine (since 3D gets much more complicated), but you might want to look into more interesting collision than just AABBs. It’s not necessary of course, I mean you can still do cool stuff like angular momentum with AABBs if you recompute the AABB every time, but it probably won’t look very good. I feel like just doing linear momentum will feel too rigid, as well.

Even though you’re using tiles, are you doing any sort of collision resolution? A better question might be whether you’re doing a discrete or continuous physics simulation. It sounds like you’re just doing the simulation part and not any of the collision or resolution (again, really depends on discrete versus continuous here). I’m just curious because I do not recall a single person every posting about physics on this forum. I actually didn’t even get into the wonderful world of physics simulations until my undergrad. It’s an awesome part of game development that is very far away from RS, to some respect (there are some valid arguments that every game requires some level of physics, but not every game requires physics simulation).

Also, car physics can be interestingly complicated to implement. There are a lot of cool resources out there for people who have made various attempts at simulating cars. I suppose it depends how realistic you want it to be. Meh.

That site seems dependable for the simulation side, but you should also look more into how physics engines are structured. The translation between physics as a mathematics and physics simulation programming is a bit of work. The mathematics gives you the tools you need to program the laws of physics, but that tends to be just one part of the physics engine (and actually is usually the easiest part if you’re working with a simple kinematics engine). Collision detection and collision resolution are much more complicated, but allow you to apply the simulation stuff in some very interesting ways (such as being able to walk around a 3D world and not walk through walls). As far as car physics goes, this is where bumping into other cards or not crashing into the walls comes in.

As far as using specialized hardware to do physics (probably a GPU), I highly recommend against this. It is much more complicated than using a GPU for graphics programming. Everything has to be framed differently when you’re trying to apply it to a GPU. You also really want to understand how a GPU is structured before trying to apply it for a GPGPU problem (since this isn’t graphics). Finally, physics on the GPU works quite nicely if your graphics is on the GPU. Physics being on the GPU but graphics on the CPU will be immensely complicated and not worth the headache (if you’re curious why, I’m happy to go into more detail as to why this is probably not a good idea).

If you are worried about performance, you can certainly multithread your engine. One thread may be responsible for physics and another for graphics. You have to worry about synchronization points and such, but this is probably a much more feasible and interesting problem for you to solve than trying to force a GPU into doing what it is not inherently designed to do. If you are really convinced you want to do physics on the GPU, then you really should learn first how to work with OpenGL and/or DirectX and gain some understanding as to how shaders work and how the GPU computing model works before delving into physics. Others may disagree and just say go ahead and learn OpenCL or CUDA, but I think for the purposes of game physics, it makes much more sense to understand how to apply the GPU for graphics first, then try and translate that knowledge over to real-time physics. OpenCL and CUDA are designed more for GPGPU problems and not necessarily for real-time problems (though they of course can be).

You do talk about a lot of very interesting topics to learn, but I think you should focus on one at a time. Some of these topics have many years of material if you really want to get into them.
