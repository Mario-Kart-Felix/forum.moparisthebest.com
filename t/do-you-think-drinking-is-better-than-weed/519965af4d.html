  1. If you play your Xbox, or a computer game, for a long time, and then you suddenly stop, in a few days, months, years, you will get cravings for the activity again. This is because the activity is psychologically addictive - you do it because it makes you happy, and your body gets addicted to the serotonin/dopamine release from this activity because your day-to-day life without the activity doesn’t have the same releases in it.
  2. So is a lot of the food that you eat and the beverages that you drink - do you think that Coca Cola has never entered a laboratory?
  3. One of the effects of marijuana is an enlargement of the bronchi in your lungs - it opens your lungs up and makes it easier for you to breathe, effectively working as a lung cleaning mechanism. This is why some runners smoke weed because they find it easier to breathe. You can also see this study from the University of California that showed that marijuana users actually had lower risk of developing lung cancer. So no, whilst it doesn’t take a rocket scientist to make an assumption, a lung scientist might be able to conduct a better study.
  4. I’m not sure what you were referring to with number 4, but if you were talking about “zero deaths in history” or “no overdose”, it is close to impossible to overdose on marijuana, and for it to cause a direct death. If you were talking about “saves police and NHS billions”, an article published in the Journal of Addictive Behaviours reported that “alcohol is clearly the drug with the most evidence to support a direct intoxication-violence relationship,” whereas “cannabis reduces the likelihood of violence during intoxication.”
  5. I don’t think you quite understand how a drug works. It doesn’t come in and rewire your brain, it mimics another chemical at the synapses to cause neurotransmitters to send out chemicals. Marijuana, in this case, mimics Anandamide (a chemical released during the early stages of pregnancy) in order to trigger the release of Dopamine (a chemical released in the pleasure and reward centres of the brain). So a drug doesn’t change the way your brain functions, it simply triggers an already-programmed function. Although some studies have shown an actual positive effect on the brain from marijuana usage.
