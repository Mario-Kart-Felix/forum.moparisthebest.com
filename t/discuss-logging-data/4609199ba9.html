I’m a little bit late to the discussion, I know (I havn’t been here since November, so I missed it). But here is what I have to say.

At a company where I worked, say 3 years ago, we logged all actions for players to log files on the local machine. There’s a reason for that. It’s much faster to log to files. Still sticking the square in the circle hole, I see. Your database should act as a single source of truth.

Consider this scenario. Three game servers, A, B, and C. For one reason or another, the NIC used for communicating with the database server on server A fails. Servers B and C are fine, and remain operational.
Several shitty outcomes:

  1. A player has played for half an hour after. They log out to take a piss and then try to login again. Failure. They go to another server, log in, and notice that they’ve lost the rare drop they had.
  2. Despite losing the drop, they continue to play on the new server and acquire an inventory full of stuff before logging out again. You merge the logs from the server with the broken link back into the database – what items does the player now hold?

Never mind the loss of faith from players that such a scenario can result in. Have you considered the technical difficulty in merging the data back? Which data takes precedence?
Robust means fault tolerant, not fault masking. A cluster failure (single database server cluster, in this case, I guess) is catastrophic, and there’s no shame in shutting the service down when a catastrophic failure occurs. You do everybody a disservice by doing it any other way - the players who perhaps lost their time, and the poor bastard who has to make the public apology.

EDIT: Also, object isn’t necessarily right about logging being inherently faster. If you’re writing a log file to its own dedicated physical disk then yeah, the writes are purely sequential (so there’s no seek) – as long as you pre-allocate the log file as one massive block on disk to avoid fragmentation.
However if the disk is being used for other IO, and that other IO is heavy enough, then you really aren’t any better off. You’ll potentially wind up with disk thrashing occurring.

So really, log writing isn’t necessarily, and certainly not always, faster. You need to design the system so that it has sufficient IO capacity.
