If you are in a life threatening situation, you get aid immediately (obviously). If not, then depending on your symptoms you might have to wait a few hours, or days if it doesn’t need immediate care. You can get free pain killers if you want, though, so the problems aren’t that big.

Besides, many diseases need testing to confirm, so you have to wait nonetheless. If you need a test that involves depositing a bottle of urine, you can do that and know the results tomorrow. A doctor doesn’t need to receive the cup in person, and he doesn’t do the tests either. Same with blood-tests. There are a lot of things people with lower educations than a doctor can do, which is cheaper and allows the doctor to do things only he can do.

If we feel ill all of the sudden, we have four options. We either call the doctor, and he’ll come by when he has time or we visit him. When it’s a bit more serious, there is a doctor waiting at the hospital, and when you give him a call you’re added to a list, and you’ll be visited the same day. You usually call this guy at night too. If you have problems that need immediate attention, you go to the ER yourself. If you feel like you are about to die, or any other serious problems, you ring 112 (European 911) and an Ambulance will arrive within 6-7 minutes.

Our waiting lists for transplants are very high, but that’s mostly due to the law and people not signing up as a donor. In the Netherlands you have to say ‘Yes I want to be a donor’, instead of the much better ‘No, I don’t want to be a donor’. But there’s also a problem with the law. Last time I checked, relatives are asked if they want to donate the organs of a (almost) deceased person. Even if the dead person enlisted as a donor. People usually say no when they have to decide over the organs of relatives.

If you need specialist surgery, and it’s not available in the Netherlands, you are allowed to go to a different country and be operated there. It’s even (partially) compensated by insurance companies.
