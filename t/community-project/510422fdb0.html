It depends entirely on your experience level. If you’ve done an extensive amount of game development before, you have an engine to work with, available assets or are able to pop out decent assets yourself, and have a clear idea of a design, then you might be able to push out a decent android game in a month or two. If you are missing one or more of those, expect a lot more time. Experience is really helpful in areas where you can avoid unnecessary pitfalls you may not have originally intended.

If you aren’t using a prebuilt engine of some kind, then you’ll have to end up writing your own. That’s cool and fun for some people, but you’ll more than likely have more fun writing the engine and just spend the entire time writing a decent engine proof, not a game. On the opposing end, the engine may be completely not fun and you may give up on the project before even reaching game development.

Finally, assets are a factor most people overlook. Even if you can pull off the game itself (which is a lot more work design-wise than you might expect), you still have to find decent sound and graphics assets from somewhere, otherwise your designs will either not be able to be created or will be filled in with nasty programmer art/sounds. Unless you are a very talented artist, musician or both, you can’t estimate the time you’ll spend making those assets outside of actually putting them in the game.

Say you know multiple people who can develop, make graphics, sounds, etc. Now you have to deal with having a balanced team, everyone being on the same page for the game idea (often done with a game design document), all the devs being on the same page (technical design document), the artists and sound designers actually knowing what they are doing and knowing what to make for you (you need to tell them exactly what you need, not ask them what to do), and having someone to manage everyone else (often a producer, who maintains a schedule and makes sure everyone else is on task–this is a full time job, not part of someone else’s role). For small teams, production is generally half of the workload of one of the team members, but this is for a VERY small team (1-3).

A lot more goes into the actual dynamics of the team for making a game than one might realize (beyond just the factors driving the game development itself).
