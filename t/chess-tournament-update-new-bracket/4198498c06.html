Just because you won doesn’t mean it was because of something you did or didn’t do. It also doesn’t mean you can’t improve, or should have done something differently. Ask any great chess player which games should be analyzed most (wins, losses, or draws)… Several have said they study the mistakes they made in their wins harder than any other game, because mistakes in losses and draws are usually easier to spot/correct. Mistakes in your wins are much harder to spot and correct, and your opponents will be analyzing those the most.

That being said, all your arguments based on ‘well, I won’ are completely invalid. Time management is an art. You need to know how your opponent thinks, and also prepare for them to play their best possible move. Every good chess resource says you should assume your opponent plays the best move, that way you’re always prepared for the worst. While thinking about the best move, you’ll very often stumble upon the lines your opponent has thought about, so it’s not a total loss, as you’ve already figured out the weakness of that line during your evaluation.

Again, you’re thinking that people make arbitrary and random moves, that are not calculated; which is as far from the truth as one can get. Also, any alternative move is inferior, and better for you, that’s the whole point of finding the best move…

No, the question has already been answered. 18 was a bad move, end of discussion. It put your opponent in a much more comfortable situation. Many people make stupid mistakes like that when they’re up, and lose the game because of it (I’ve seen it many, many times; I’ve also done it quite a few times). With the mentality of making inferior moves because you feel like it you’ll never improve, especially when you refuse to correct your errors because you were lucky enough to win.

It seems like you’re hinting at a set destiny type universe, and that if you made another move you would have changed said destiny, so you don’t like to disturb it. While I understand that view, it’s still not the correct way to become a better player.

You’re also saying it’s pure engine vs engine, which it’s not. I’ve been going over lesser moves as well, because that’s how you should be analyzing the game. Furthermore, every possible combination does not need to be covered… Look into how chess engines work for more information. The possibilities are greatly reduced because many moves are bad and don’t need to be considered at that time; when the bad moves are made then they can be acted up.

Winning is the biggest learning opportunity (losing reveals the most obvious and serious flaws), and it doesn’t tell you everything you did was right. All it tells you is that you didn’t make as many/bad of mistakes as your opponent.

Chess isn’t a game of chance… If this was a luck based game, then I’d tend to agree with you, but every move can be quantified, and all possibilities can be covered (most moves can be discarded because they are obviously bad, and will end in a loss).
