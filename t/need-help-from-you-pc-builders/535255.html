The link that you posted requires me to log in, and I really don’t feel like making an account, but I’ll try and help anyway.

To answer some of your questions:

  • What type of wireless card do you have? If it’s a PCIe, just make sure your new motherboard will have an available PCIe slot. Remember that your graphics card needs a spot, too.

  • When buying a motherboard, it doesn’t come with a CPU fan. Instead, you get a CPU fan when buying the CPU. Just make sure the motherboard your buying has the same socket type as the CPU you have right now, and the CPU fan should fit onto the mobo.

  • Sound is usually always integrated into the mobo, you don’t need to buy a card for it.

To install the new motherboard you will need a screw driver that isn’t magnetic, and an antistatic wrist strap (or you could just touch the metal part of the case before touching any components, which reduces the ESD). Unhook everything from the motherboard you have right now and just leave it all in the case. Take out all the screws that is attaching the mobo to the case and keep them safe. After your old mobo is out of the case, remove the CPU and DO NOT touch any of the gold pins on it. Place the CPU into your new motherboard which you should have sitting out already. To make sure the CPU fits, you need to align pin 1 (there should be a notch on the corner of the CPU that denotes a missing pin) and place it accordingly into the socket. After closing the locking assembly (should be an arm or some kind of lever), you will probably need to apply more thermal compound. Make sure the compound you apply is a thin but even coat. After that, attach the CPU heatsink/fan onto the mobo and try not to move the thermal compound around too much. Put the new mobo into your case, making sure it is secure to the side of the case. Hook everything back up and you’re good to go.

Changing out parts is extremely easy, you shouldn’t have any trouble doing it. If you need any help with it just ask, or better yet look up a guide online that explains it better. The only problem most people have when switching out a part is plugging the wires back into the same spots on the new mobo, which really isn’t hard at all.

I hope I helped.
