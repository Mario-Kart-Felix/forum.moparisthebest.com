March 2 (Bloomberg) -- President Barack Obama called a college student to offer her his support after radio commentator Rush Limbaugh attacked her for advocating health insurance coverage for contraception.

Limbaugh’s comments also drew a rebuke from Republican House Speaker John Boehner of Ohio.

Obama spoke with the student, Sandra Fluke, for several minutes, White House press secretary Jay Carney told reporters today.

“He wanted to offer his support to her,” Carney said, adding that Obama considers Limbaugh’s comments “reprehensible.” The president thanked Fluke for speaking out and expressed his disappointment that she was personally attacked, he said.

“The fact that our political discourse has become debased in many ways is bad enough,” Carney said. “It is worse when it’s directed at a private citizen who was simply expressing her views on a matter of public policy.”

On his March 1 show, Limbaugh said that Fluke, a Georgetown University law school student, is a “slut” and a “prostitute,” according to a transcript posted on his website. Today, he said on his show that Fluke “admits to having so much sex that she can’t afford it anymore. And thus, a new welfare entitlement must be created so that society will pay for it.”

Quicken Loans Inc. of Detroit suspended advertising on the Limbaugh show due to his “inflammatory comments along with valuable feedback from our clients and staff,” chairman Dan Gilbert, the owner of the Cleveland Cavaliers basketball team, said on Twitter.

All-Male Panel

Fluke was barred from testifying last month before a House committee on what became an all-male panel talking about women’s access to birth control. She appeared a week later before House Democrats and discussed the need for such access.

During their phone conversation, “he encouraged me and thanked me for speaking out about the concerns of American women,” Fluke said in an interview with MSNBC.

“What was really personal for me was that he said to tell my parents that they should be proud,” Fluke said.

Republican presidential candidate Rick Santorum, asked about Limbaugh’s comments in a CNN interview, said the talk show host was “being absurd.”

“But that’s, you know, an entertainer can be absurd,” Santorum, a former Pennsylvania senator, said. “He’s in a very different business than I am.”

Fundraising Appeal

Santorum rival Mitt Romney, a former Massachusetts governor, walked by the camera without responding when a reporter asked about the Limbaugh controversy, CNN reported.

The Democratic Congressional Campaign Committee sent a fundraising appeal shortly after Fluke was excluded from the House hearing.

Boehner criticized Limbaugh through a spokesman, Michael Steel.

“The speaker obviously believes the use of those words was inappropriate, as is trying to raise money off the situation,” Steel said.

In an e-mail to supporters, House Democratic leader Nancy Pelosi of California decried the “vicious and inappropriate attacks” leveled against Fluke.

The controversy over birth control arose after the Obama administration initially required religiously affiliated universities and hospitals to pay for contraception coverage for their employees, with the exception of houses of worship. The administration then modified the rule so that insurers, not the institutions themselves, would pay for the coverage.

The Democratic-controlled Senate yesterday blocked Republican-sponsored legislation that would let any employer or insurer exclude coverage for any health service that violates their religious beliefs.
