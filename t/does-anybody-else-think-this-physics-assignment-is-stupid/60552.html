BobDole81 July 30, 2016, 5:17am #7

There’s a book called “On the Shoulders of Giants” compiled by Stephen Hawking…it’s basically a collection of the works with biographical commentaries of some of the greatest physicists of the age of modern science. That may be worth going through (if you can find it at your library) to find a physicist whose work/life you find interesting if you haven’t already.

I guess if you’re just interested in actual quantitative physics, learning the history of it is not satisfying…I really have an interested in learning about the old, famous mathematicians and physicists, but that’s just me. You don’t really learn concepts or how to work out problems, but you gain a sense of how people were able to come up with such amazing theories and how they were able to apply the knowledge of their time to advance scientific knowledge…that is worth something if you go to work in that field.
