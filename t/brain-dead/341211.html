abraham2119 July 31, 2016, 4:40pm #1

We were debating this issue in school a while ago, and an interesting discussion came about.

Wikipedia definition:

Brain death is a legal indicator of death in the USA that refers to the irreversible end of all brain activity (including involuntary activity necessary to sustain life) due to total necrosis of the cerebral neurons following loss of blood flow and oxygenation. Brain-stem death (not whole brain death) is taken to be the significant indicator of death. The significant point is that the brain is no longer capable of sustaining the rest of the body's systems without advanced life support. It should not be confused with a persistent vegetative state. The concept of brain death emerged in the 1960s, as the ability to resuscitate individuals and mechanically keep the heart and lungs functioning became prevalent.
In other words, a perfectly functioning body with perfectly functioning organs with a seemingly unresponsive brain (determined after only a couple of hours...).

I personally believe it’s a bullshit excuse to discontinue life support and harvest the organs while they’re fresh. There have been countless cases of people who have been declared “brain-dead”, yet awake from their coma (or whatever other vegetative state) with functional brain response after a short period of time.

In class, people mentioned that there have been cases of women declared “brain-dead” giving birth while in that state. If they’re dead, how the hell is that possible? Also, in order to remove all organs from the body, the procedure is preceded by anesthetic treatment - if the brain is dead, and the nerves of the body are dead, why is it that we sedate the so-called dead patients before performing this procedure?

What do you think?
