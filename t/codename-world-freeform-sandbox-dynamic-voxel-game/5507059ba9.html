I’ve been working on water. It’s been… frustrating. However, it’s coming along fairly well. The water mesh has its own vertex buffer which will allow for fancy shaders like reflective water, refractions, etc, the terrain mesh does not connect to the water but the water mesh does (how it should be), and water mesh is not generated against the terrain, so it’s optimal in terms of triangles generated. This part was extremely hard to figure out, since marching cubes works on an “inside/outside” principle. While air and inside terrain both constituted as outside of water, I had to determine what kind of outside the vertices were. And it wasn’t as simple as if air, it’s outside and terrain it’s inside. This would work for preventing water mesh from generating against terrain mesh and only against air, but it also wouldn’t connect, which was a major problem. Another obvious solution is to only generate water if there’s air above it, but this won’t work for waterfalls or pouring water.

The solution was simple and elegant. Air and terrain were treated as outside of water, but the vertices could only be used if at least one of the 3 of 12 modified vertices was an air element. I’m doing a poor job of explaining it, but it’s the proper solution and fits perfectly into marching cubes.


You’ll notice the water sticks out of the ground and goes down when connecting. Like I said, it’s a WIP. I sort of like the look though, so I’m not entirely sure I’ll change it. Also the water element is, of course, temporary and just for testing.

So I haven’t posted an update in a while, but that’s because I’ve been optimizing and improving world loading. I’d get occasional crashes sometimes, framerates would be unstable and drop to a few hundred frames per second and rise again, world loading would sometimes cause stuttering, chunks would rarely not have their mesh render and have to be manually set as dirty, water data would sometimes be deleted twice, and there would be the occasional memory leak. But I’ve been spending the past few days doing lots of annoying work to make the game as stable and smooth as possible.

All of the issues were related to thread synchronization. I’ve made some minor design changes and fixed small errors in old code that functioned properly but required a cheap fix to cover up their mistakes. It’s been stressful and tedious. I’ve also been doing very dirty tricks to eliminate all stutters and optimize framerates. Such tricks are having queues for queues for queues and dynamically allocating memory on the stack. Yes, on the stack, via alloca.

That no doubt sounds stupid, but it nearly eliminates all resource contention and optimizes cache hits. For the queues, I’ll use an example. The first one is used in the main thread to find chunks that need to have their mesh updated, and adds them to the list. At the end of the loop for checking, they’re all added to the second queue. This requires locking the second queue. The third queue is used strictly in the mesh thread, which adds the second queue’s contents to it and sorts them via distance to the camera.

With that 3-queue system, the main thread doesn’t have to wait on the sorting by the third thread or consistently lock the second queue, or even keep the second queue locked so the mesh thread has to wait for the main thread to finish its updating. I know the performance difference isn’t huge, but I want to squeeze every bit of performance out of this engine to make it run on as low-end hardware as possible.

The dynamic stack allocations is because accessing memory on the heap thousands of times per second isn’t going to be as fast as accessing memory on the stack, and dynamically allocating memory on the heap is very costly if you have other threads trying to do it. So previously in the render loop, it would loop through all of the chunks (heap memory and not cache-friendly), see if they could be rendered, and then render them. Just accessing the chunks alone was expensive, so I was forced to create a class that contained the variables necessary for rendering a chunk, which only consisted of 20 or so bytes, and use them instead. However, I guess there were still lots of cache misses because profiling showed accessing them was the single most expensive thing in the render loop - something not even rendering related! So since the class is small, I’m able to copy all of them onto the stack and have cache hits much more likely. Coincidentally, doing this stabilized the framerate and profiling showed the DirectX functions were the most expensive in the function, as it should be.

It’s become clear now just why C++ is the preferred language for game development. I don’t know if these kinds of optimizations are as easily doable in C# and Java, or other high-level languages. Or maybe I’m just a bad programmer and don’t actually know what I’m doing. I just go by what I’ve read, what the profiler tells me, and how the game runs.

TL;DR: The game is faster and bug-free. It runs on average at a stable ~3000 FPS, dropping to a minimum of 1000 when lots of the world is generating. Here are some pictures of experimental “cave” generation with water and then pee-like light-emitting water.

Alright, so even without waves, the water is still bumpy. This is, unfortunately, due to how I’m currently handling mesh generation. I’ve been trying to perfect it for over a week, but to keep my sanity, I’ve determined to just pick something that fits most of the following:

[ul][li]Doesn’t generate past terrain[/li]
[li]Flat still water[/li]
[li]Y coordinates is respective to liquid level[/li]
[li]Flowing water connects to still water[/li]
[li]Flowing water has volume[/li]
[li]Waves and animations don’t cause gaps between the terrain[/li]
[li]No natural gaps between terrain[/li]
[li]Not restricted to squares[/li][/ul]

I’ve experimented with several options - 4 in fact, and none of them fit more than 3 of the above. It’s a huge task and I didn’t expect it to be easy, but I also didn’t expect it to be this challenging. I’ve been at it for weeks with little to show from when I started besides shaders, and for now, like I said, I’m taking the easy way out that suits the game in a charming way and makes bumps look okay when water is wavy. Unfortunately, still water height isn’t respective to the liquid level without making the flowing water appear flat, but I’m sure I can find a fix for this. It’s just very difficult and trust me, I’m trying to make it look realistic and satisfy all the requirements above. I don’t like bumpy water anymore than you guys do.

You can choose which you want to use in-game. Interpolated is more expensive because it requires more sampling, and personally I dislike it because of vertex blending artifacts. However…

I’ve spent the past 2 days improving smooth shading. I’ve optimized the number of samples taken for lighting and normals so it’s almost as fast as blocky shading. Not only that, but I also implemented an experimental feature for helping solve the vertex blending artifacts by rendering the lights to a texture, blurring that texture, and overlaying it on top of the scene. It really knocks down the framerate depending on the variable strength of the blur, and it does introduce artifacts on edges, but it REALLY makes lighting look so much better.
