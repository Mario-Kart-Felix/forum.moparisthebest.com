My Lawnmower

A few months ago, I was making an air engine in engineering class at school and I decided to make something bigger and better, that could run on steam. After a few minutes searching for steam engines, I noticed that you could convert an old lawn mower to run on air. So I went to the shed and found an old dead lawn mower, stripped it down and cleaned all the internals. After I took the head off, the gasket broke, so it was leaking air all over the place. I decided to try epoxy glue as a head gasket. This worked very well and stopped all leaks. I then took the old cam out and welded two new lobes on to the opposite sides that the original ones were on, this created a 4 lobed cam, making the engine into a two-stroke. I then ran air into the engine through where the carburettor used to be and it ran ‘alrightish’ - about 200 rpm. After that, I found an old pipe lying around which had a 70mm interior diameter, I cut that down to 350mm and found a big sheet of 5mm steel plate. Using a plasma cutter, I cut them into circles, then used the grinder to make them smooth. I then spent some time welding them to the ends of the pipe (I drilled an outlet hole on the pipe first so it wouldn’t explode.) Once it was welded and I was happy that there were no leaks, I welded a small pipe onto the outlet hole so I could hook on the steam delivery hose. I then made a flange for the input (old exhaust) port on the engine, again, I used epoxy glue to seal the gaps. Then I gave the engine a test run, it ran fairly slowly even though the fire was about 3 times hotter than the one in this video. In my attempts to make the original cam work better, I managed to snap it in the middle. So that gave me a bit of a problem to solve. I returned to the shed and found another dead lawn mower. I dismantled the engine and, to my surprise, the gear and the lobes of the cam were made of plastic. I tried to super glue layers of plastic on to make new lobes, but they kept ripping off, I had to find a better way to make it work. I took the cam to school, and after a few minutes talking to the teacher, I decided to get some 25mm mild steel bar and turn it to the correct sizes in the lathe. Once I finally managed to get the bar to the right size,I drilled a half inch hole through the middle. Then I used the milling machine to flatten off opposing sides. It then started to resemble a cam. There was still a lot of work to be done. I then used the bench grinder to roughly shape the lobes. Then, with the angle grinder, I made the lobes a bit more correct in shape. After that, I spent about 4 or 5 hours fine tuning the cam, modifying the con-rod, and positioning it on the cam shaft. It could still be slightly better, but right now it is running very well. Also, the engine runs backwards now, I cannot rotate the cam 90 degrees because it then hits the con-rod. So I just welded some sharp plates onto the blade so it cuts when running in reverse. (About 2000 RPM at 100 PSI of air, and it idles at about 200 RPM at 20 PSI (through a small jet, it might go faster with a bigger hose.) So yea, it can cut a little bit of grass how it is at the moment running on steam. The next thing will be to make a better boiler and a more efficient firebox. I might also put a barbecue on it so you can make lunch while mowing the lawns.

All in all, the engine has been opened over 100 times in the last two months.
