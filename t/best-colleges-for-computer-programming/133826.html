BobDole81 July 30, 2016, 1:40pm #7

I was accepted into CMU (College of Sciences, not CS), but I didn’t try MIT. CMU’s school of Computer Science is one of the best in the country. Spaces are extremely limited and there is huge competition to get in. In my ignorance, in my application I put down the school of CS as my second choice (as I didn’t know what else to choose), and I was immediately rejected As for MIT, a family friend of ours did his graduate work at MIT and recommended against going there, saying that students are so overworked that undergrads literally have no life beyond school.

Another family friend recommended against going to Harvard (where he attended for undergrad), or any of the highfalutin ivy leagues (and their competitors) unless you absolutely know what you want to do. If you are decided on the relative path that you want to take for your degree and career, then those colleges will be able to provide countless opportunities. But if you’re relatively undecided, he said, then you really won’t enjoy it.

BobDole81 July 30, 2016, 1:40pm #9
xxlegitxx:

I thought Princeton didn’t allow you to pick a major until junior year?

I don’t remember mentioning Princeton

But when applying for colleges, you do have to put down a tentative major (most colleges have an undecided option). The first year, I believe, is mostly spent doing general courses and courses of personal interest…Cornell emphasizes the latter, and recommends going through their course catalog and just picking random courses that appeal to you for the first year.

What I meant about knowing what you want to do is that if you have a major/career plan in mind, you can choose the major you are going to stick with earlier on, and you can take advantage of internship, co-op, and research opportunities earlier as opposed to waiting when you do know what to do. For most colleges, I would imagine that this is not a big deal - you have a few years to make up your mind and decided what to do. But the upper colleges have such a competitive student body, that it is apparently difficult to do that (as I mentioned, this is what I was told…this part is not from experience).
