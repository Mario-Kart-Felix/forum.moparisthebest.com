What is truth or 'historical fact'?

General Discussion
davidi2 August 4, 2016, 5:43am #1

Philosophers have reached different conclusions when trying to explain the nature of knowledge and truth. Is truth objective, in the sense that it’s independent of our personal opinions, and something that we discover rather than create or invent; or is it relative to the conceptual system of an individual or an entire culture, so that what qualifies as a fact or a true statement may differ depending on one’s point of view, goals, cultural beliefs, or language? Those who believe in objective facts tend to hold the correspondence theory of the nature of truth, while relativists tend to hold a pragmatic or coherence theory. As an example, consider the controversy over celebrating one of the US national holidays: Columbus Day. In the past, school textbooks taught that Christopher Columbus was a great and admirable hero who, out of a determination to prove that the world was round, “discovered America” and began a process of making the benefits of civilization and Christianity available to millions of people. On the other hand, more recent historians have studied the records of the time and concluded that on the contrary, Columbus was motivated almost entirely by greed for gold, and ruthlessly enforced policies that subjected hundreds of thousands of indigenous people to slavery, torture, and mass murder. What are the “true facts”; and what should we be teaching students in school textbooks?

Here is a recent news article about decisions reached by a Texas board of education to revise textbooks intended for use in American history classes:

The Independent – 28 Mar 10

'Texas schoolbook massacre' rewrites American history

Country music is an important modern cultural movement; hip-hop isn't. Thomas Jefferson deserves to be erased from a list of "great Americans", but Ronald Reagan doesn't. And we should re-evaluate Senator Joe McCarthy: he was almost certainly a...

Notice that many of the revisions have been strenuously opposed by historians on the grounds that they are “inaccurate”—that is, they don’t correspond to the (objective) facts. On the other hand, comments by board members who made the revisions seem to echo Nietzsche’s claim that “there are no facts, only interpretations”, and perhaps William James’ view that the truth is “what works” best for us. An additional theory that seems very relevant here is that of Foucault, who argued that what constitutes knowledge, truth, and facts, is power: that if you want to understand the nature of knowledge in specific situations, you should study politicians rather than philosophers. In the news article, however, the chairman of the Council for History Education says that the textbook decisions aren’t a political issue but a question of “good history.”

So here’s the debate:

Do you agree with the historians opposing the textbook revisions that there are objective facts of history that we can discover by studying the available evidence, and that we should try to ensure that history texts correspond with those facts as closely as possible? What is your assessment of the alternative views that no such "objective" truths or facts exist?

Should "historical accuracy" (correspondence to the facts) be the main consideration in designing school history texts, or are other criteria more important?—for example, in the case of Columbus, should books ignore or downplay his greed and cruelty for the sake of telling a story aimed at engendering patriotism? In your answer, explain whether or not you think that as a student in school you "have a right to learn the truth."

When I think of the term ‘truth’, I usually categorize events into that category if they are witnessed by myself and no one else. Nothing is ‘true’ to me unless I have the ability to witness it in full perception, so that there can be no questioning about what happened in that event, even the smallest things.

When I think of the term ‘historical’, I usually judge this by a non-biased description of something. Think of any war that has ever occurred; I would be more likely to believe a phrase along the lines of “The United States entered WWI after being heavily pressured by the Lusitania incident”, rather than “The United States entered WWI due to many different radical views of politicians upon the many events involving threats against the United States itself”.

In other words, I don’t believe a historical event based on the reasoning of humans, but rather information about the plot of the event. People mix and match words very easily, and the smallest change in a statement can make the event being described turn into something completely different, which is also why I don’t believe in a broad description of something, such as dates or other numbers relating to specific occurrences in the event.

When we talk about truth in the context of history, we’re talking about something we believe to be true, we use evidence that has been drawn from primary and secondary sources however much of it is the result of speculation.

What’s considered factually correct and incorrect has been the subject of many philosophers; Rene Descartes for instance set out to find what he could and could not prove after he deemed his formal education to be a failure because he wasn’t sure which knowledge he had been taught was correct and which was not; this stemmed from the Scientific Revolution, when Galileo proved that the Sun was the centre of the Universe because of the motion of one of the moons orbiting Jupiter, this contradicted the Bible and thus for Descartes questioned his entire formal education, how can he trust what he is being taught if later on his life he is told it is a lie?

That’s the problem with facts, and largely the problem with scientific methodology, since facts are always open to change and aren’t really fixed quantities, i.e they are falsifiable they usually become obsolete.

This is personally my entire gripe with the current education system, we’re taught everything as a collection of facts, facts that no doubt will be obsolete in the near future. On the matter of history, history in the its true form is merely a story; one cannot accurately portray every event that happened from every possible narrative and thus relies heavily on evidence, but even then new evidence comes into light and even proves old evidence to be false, to give you a very good example Lorenzo Valla was an Italian humanist during the period of the Renaissance, Lorenzo Valla used his linguistic-al knowledge to deduce that an important primary source: The Donation of Constantine was actually a forgery because the language used in that context would have been much different to that originally used (i.e the Latin had a different structure).

The point i’m trying to make here is that the Donation of Constantine (which it is widely believed it was composed around the 8th century) had been considered a fact, a solid primary source for around 7 centuries, and no one knew anything about it they widely regarded it as a fact.
