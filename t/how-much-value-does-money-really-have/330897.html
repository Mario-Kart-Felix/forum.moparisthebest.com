R.I.P_RSC July 31, 2016, 2:58pm #7

Money is exactly what Unhandled and Eh Pretty Easy say. First, it replaces a lot of trade-values with a ‘third-party’. Say you have a cow, and you need carrots. You can trade the cow for the carrots, but you can also use money. That way you don’t have to buy a cow’s worth of carrots, but exactly the amount you need and you end up with carrots and spare money. Money also allows you to consider time. What if carrots are sold in June, but my cow is ready for the butcher in February? I want those carrots, but I can’t trade my cow for it.

Money equals trust. The first currency’s were pebbles and seashells. Years later, metals such as gold and silver were used as coins. These coins have a real value, which only fluctuates when the price of the metal they were made of fluctuates. But then there were banks. You don’t want to walk around with lots of gold coins in your pockets, so banks would store them for you. You’d get a certificate that said that you were the owner of X gold coins. After a while, those certificates became notes with fixed values (different per bank). You could use those banknotes to buy stuff. But those notes were so convenient, that hardly anyone would claim the gold.

That was when banks started to create money. People didn’t have to bring gold to the bank, but would take a loan. A debt. At the same time, the bank was in debt to the borrower. The banknotes equal a certain amount of gold, so the bank would have to give gold to the borrower, if he wanted it. But the money was created, so there is no gold. So you have to trust the bank, because if the bank goes bankrupt, you have a bunch of notes that are worth nothing. This is a mutual debt (is that the correct term?)

So there you go. A short history lesson, and the reason why we can’t get rid of money in our society.
