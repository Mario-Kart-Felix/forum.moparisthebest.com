Just to continue on the discussion about monarchies: Our queen has one task when it comes to law-making. She has to sign a bill when it passed the lower and upper houses. If she doesn’t want to, our minister of justice can sign it. So our queen doesn’t have any influence on our laws at all. She does appoint the people that lead the formation of a new government, but obviously the parties will decide who will form the government. Our queen isn’t responsible for her actions either, our Prime Minister is.

I’m not sure whether I’m pro- or anti-monarchy. Our queen is a decent representative of our nation, and is well known by heads of state around the world. But people like Merkel or Obama are good representatives too (Sarkozy and Berlusconi are bad for their countries’ reputation, but they can’t be blamed, their French/Italian).

@Frell: Obama was the lesser of two evils. You’ll probably disagree, but a Republican government would and will be bad for your reputation, and the future of your country.

About systems like ‘the winner takes all’: These systems have a few upsides and a downside. The upsides are that the majority agrees with the winner, forming a government doesn’t take long and there is no need to compromise. The downside is that the minority isn’t that minor. Theoretically you could have a result of 50.1% of the votes for the winner, and 49.9% for the loser. Especially when you have a two party system like in the US, the two parties will never agree. One day you will have to compromise, but then you get “it’s their idea so we say nay”. Everyone should have the ability to found a party, and every party should have a realistic chance to get at least one seat in parliament. In countries like the US and England you have to join an existing party to be elected, so the right to be elected in those countries is only theoretical.
