They are the same thing. Since “atomic bomb” was used in the 1940s it tends to be associated with the earliest pure fission weapons (of course, this is just from an American perspective - e.g. 30s-40s German physicists tended to use the term “uranium bomb”). The bombs dropped on Hiroshima and Nagasaki in 1945 are generally called atomic bombs.

I assume that the use of “nuclear bomb” now is either a slightly more accurate name or a misused abbreviation of “thermonuclear”. Thermonuclear refers strictly to the fusion weapons that make up most modern arsenals. Even then it’s not entirely accurate, since as far as I know all fusion weapons are initiated by a smaller fission explosion (it’s called staged radiation implosion, invented in the early 1950s).

Ok, enough lecture (nuclear weapons are interesting), if you’re interested here’s a great site.

The question was whether or not we could destroy all life on the planet with a nuclear war. The answer is "probably"

We could irradiate the entire surface of the planet, no doubt about it. Detonate several large salted bombs (designed for extra fallout) high enough in the atmosphere and air currents would spread the debris very, very far away from the detonation site.

The US and various states of the former USSR possess more than enough weapons to accomplish this. The fact that most of the active ones are aimed at centers of population, utilities, and infrastructure means that most humans would likely die in the initial blasts. If that were followed up with the salted bombs I mentioned earlier, probably all higher mammals and most reptiles would die soon after.

There might be survivors (anyone far enough underground, naturally resistant animals, etc). If they managed to find food and avoid lethal doses of radiation, a species might eventually evolve that isn’t killed by radiation. Maybe. And it would take a very long time.
