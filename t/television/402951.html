You are also aware that plasma TVs burn out and can’t be repaired after a certain period of time, which is generally very short. Also, you have to realize refresh rate doesn’t matter on a TV like it does on a computer monitor. Almost all TV content is broadcast at somewhere around 30hz (NTSC is that, PAL might be slightly different), if you are watching content that has been recorded at 30hz or so, it doesn’t matter if your TV does that or better, if your TV does 30hz or 3000hz you will see the same thing, no difference at all. Lately, some bluray content supports higher refresh rates, but your eye isn’t good enough to tell the difference past a certain point, so you are just throwing your money away.

edit:

There are several new technologies that have helped to increase the lifespan of plasma televisions. First we have the pixel orbiter. The orbiter quickly swaps the color of adjacent pixels when a static image is being displayed. This creates a sense of picture change for the television itself but it practically impossible for the viewer to see. This almost single-handedly eliminates the threat of burn-in. Another software component of "Anti-Burn" technology searches out pixels that have been on too long and shuts them off for a short period of time to allow them to rest. These advantages prevent burn-in and, by attending to pixel health, extend the lifespan of plasma televisions.

Before even going into what type of TV would be the best for you, what are you going to be using it for mostly? And at what time during the day do you most watch TV or play videogames? Nowadays all of these things are factors, a big one being energy saving, so you’ll keep more money in your pocket if you plan on keeping your TV on for a large percentage of the time.

The only real difference nowadays, between LCD and plasma is the heat the produce and the amount of energy they produce. Since the picture of plasma TV’s are created from burning phosphors, they obviously generate a lot more heat, and require more energy to run. Since I don’t presume altitude will be an issue for you (plasma’s have been known to have a slightly degraded performance at higher altitudes), either TV is an option for you.

However if you do end up ruling plasma out do to glare, or any other reason, definitely go with LED. Although thinness isn’t a big deal the colors will be much crisper and clearer on an LED TV as apposed to an LCD. Not to mention your energy consumption will be a lot less.

I was talking about burn out, not burn in, they are two completely different things. Burn in is when you see a ‘shadow’ of something that had been left too long on your screen, it messes with your picture, and looks bad, but as you say has been almost completely eliminated in the past few years. (but if you are concerned, technologies like DLP don’t suffer from burn-in). Burn out is just like it sounds, when a light bulb burns out, you throw it away because it is useless, when a plasma TV burns out, you also throw it away because it is useless, and can’t be repaired. (As opposed to DLP, where a bulb can burn out, but it can also be replaced to return the TV to as good as new).
