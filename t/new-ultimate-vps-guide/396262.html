Welcome to my VPS guide! In this tutorial I will explain the following things:

[ul][b][list][li]What a VPS is.[/li]
[li]How to obtain a VPS[/li]
[li]How to connect to your VPS[b][/li]
[/list]

Before you even start, you need to know what a VPS is. Think of a VPS as your own computer, except with no programs running that give you your everyday enjoyment. A VPS, infact, is a “computer” that can be used for many different reasons (in our case, hosting your RSPS). Hosting is the main thing these servers are used for.

I will only be discussing things that have to deal with Windows VPS’s and Linux. I DO NOT recommend any other OS (operating system), because most are complicated, or do not support Java.

Step 1:
Obtaining a VPS -
This is probably going to be one of the hardest steps in this whole guide. The reason why: It is getting harder and harder to find cheap and good VPS’s. The one VPS provider I do NOT recommend is www.VPSLand.com. VPSLand is run by a group of teenagers (or atleast people that act like it) and business there is usually slow. I do not have any VPS provider recommendation, because most VPS that are good, are to expensive for starting a server. You can search for VPS providers on www.google.com though.

Step 2:
Finding out of the VPS you want to go with is right for you -
Once you actually find a provider you think you want to purchase a VPS from, you need to take a few key recommendations into consideration:[list]
[li]How is the provider’s Support/Contact system?[/li]
[li]Check the VPS’s specs, or in other words, DO NOT purchase a VPS that doesn’t state what the computer specs are.[/li]
[/list]
Here is the recommended VPS specs for a starting server:[list]
[li]RAM: 512MB-1GB (This is the most important factor in my opinion)[/li]
[li]Disk Space:5GB-10GB (If you can obtain a VPS with a better disk space, thats great, but keep in mind, this isn’t a storage device. You probably won’t even use over 2 GB of this as a starting server.[/li]
[li]Bandwidth: 250GB-500GB[/li]
[li]Internet Speed: Above 10 Mbps. (I recommend 100 Mbps, and this is the most used by VPS providers. The higher your Mbps is, the more players you can hold, PLUS faster file/program downloading.[/li]
[/list]

Step 3:
Connecting to your VPS -
When it comes to Windows VPS’s, the most used method of connecting to your VPS, is RDC (Remote Desktop Connection), but some providers use Plesk, which I don’t recommend, as RDC is the most user-friendly way of connecting. If your provider’s VPS’s don’t support RDC, I suggest finding a new provider (unless you like Plesk). I will only discuss how to connect using RDC below:

Remote Desktop Connection(RDC):
First, you will want to click your start button, then click accessories, and then finally click Remote Desktop Connection. After that you will want to click the “Options >>” tab in the bottom right corner of the RDC. You will see two parts to fill in information: “Computer” and “Username”.

The Computer is the part that you type your VPS’s IP into, and the Username is usually Administrator (this varies throughout every VPS provider, so once you receive your e-mail from the provider your purchased your VPS from, it will tell you all the information for this). After you have typed in that info, you will click “Connect” and then you can start transferring your server files to your new VPS!

Connect to a Linux VPS(This is very complicated, and takes time to get used to):
SSH (Secure SHell) is normally used to allow secure encrypted command line connections to a Linux server. It can also be used to create encrypted network tunnels to transport unencrypted data, such as email sessions, FTP, etc… It provides the best security when accessing another computer remotely…

Here are the most simple steps on connecting to your Linux server using an SSH Client, PuTTY:

PuTTY is a client program for the SSH, Telnet and Rlogin network protocols. These protocols are all used to run a remote session on a computer, over a network…
In other words, you run PuTTY on a Windows machine, to connect to a *nix machine which is accessible over the network.
** Download the PuTTY client from HERE.
** Run the EXE by double clicking on it.
** Enter your server’s IP in the hostname/IP section [as shown in the below figure]…

** Change the SSH port from the default [22] only if the SSH daemon on the server is set to run on a different one & press the Open button.
** Enter root as user name & press ENTER.
** Now enter root password [it won’t be shown as a security measure] & press ENTER.
You can get the complete usages about any commands using the man command, you can type “man command”, and you will be given a manual page on how the command is used and what it does…
