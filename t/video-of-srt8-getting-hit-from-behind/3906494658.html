The bodies of cars used to be made of out hard, rigid steel and when impacted, all of the force would be transfered to the passengers causing whiplash and other related injuries. Then car companies created what is called the crumple zone and the rigid passenger cell. The crumple zone is that part of the car that is intended to crumple up and absorb most of the impact, acting as a cushion. The rigid passenger cell is the area where people inside of the car are seated. You would still want this “box” to be made out of hard, rigid steel, obviously. If it were to crumple as the front and rear of a car is designed to, the passengers would be most likely killed. Here’s a diagram for you people that are more visual learners.
