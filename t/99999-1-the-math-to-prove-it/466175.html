Does .999 (continued) = 1?

There are a lot of people that say yes, and also a lot of people who would say no. There is no right or wrong answer, just debate the best you can.

This is what I can understand about this topic…

Most common reasons people get confused:

[ol][li]You might think every number has a unique decimal expansion, you would be wrong.[/li]
[li]You might think infinitely small numbers like (0.000…01) exist, they don’t.[/li]
[li]You might think 0.999… terminates, it doesn’t.[/li][/ol]

I’ll prove that 0.999… equals 1 three different ways for you guys.

Firstly, take a real life scenario, if you were somehow to be exact with measuring…
Take a bottle of water, pour it into three glasses… So that the amount of water is exactly the same in each glass (if this was possible). You know that 1/3 is 0.333…, and that three sets of these will be 0.999… However, this came from 1 bottle, so you must know that 0.999… = 1.

Second way is simple math by induction…
x = 0.999…
10x = 9.999…
10x-x = 9.999… - 0.999…
9x = 9
x = 1

But…
x = 0.999…

Meaning…
1 = 0.999…

Thirdly, this gets into more complex math, which some of you wouldn’t understand… This also deals with the convergence theorum…

Firstly, we define 0.999… as an infinite series:
0.999… = 0 + 9(1/10) + 9(1/10)^2 + 9(1/10)^3 + 9(1/10)^4 + …

Then we use the convergence theorum (look it up if you don’t know what it is):
ar + ar^2 + ar^3 + … = ar/(1-r)

9(1/10) + 9(1/10)^2 + 9(1/10)^3 + … =
9(1/10) / 1-(1/10) = 1

After the math, 1 = 0.999…

davidi2 August 4, 2016, 12:58am #4

There is no debate, it’s simple limit math.

When you add the repetition modifier to a decimal, it’s a sign that means you are finding the limit of the fractional equivalent to the decimal. People use this because decimals are not always 100% accurate (1/3, etc)

The simplest way to prove it’s one is like this:

x = 0.999…
10x = 9.999…
-x -x (because x = 0.999…)
9x = 9
x = 1

The common misconception is that the 0.999… simply means you are putting any number of 9’s in a row (its what I thought at first), but that’s not correct.
