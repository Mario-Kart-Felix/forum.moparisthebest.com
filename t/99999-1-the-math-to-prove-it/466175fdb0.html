mod_taharok August 4, 2016, 1:12am #76

This is obvious for two different categories of analysis.

If you analyze it discreetly, then you run into an issue. As you continue to get smaller and smaller fractions, that is, 9(1/(10^x)), and are applying this to finite and measurable properties (such as mass or atoms or something), you’ll eventually hit a known limitation in our understanding of physics and won’t be able to go any farther. This holds true if you are storing such an instance on a computer, since a computer does not have infinite memory. This being said, it goes back to the concept of precision when analyzing this discreetly. To counteract the discreet measurement, we round up to verify precision, since we already know the smaller a fraction gets, the less impact a rounding will have on the result. Another way to think of it is, the more fractional 9s the number has, the more like 1 it becomes. Okay, cool.

Using infinitesimals, on the other hand, is cooler. Why? No physical limitations. We can truly represent a number which goes on forever. Fortunately, our logic from the last sentence of the discreet analysis also holds true, here. As the OP said, if we pack the number into an infinite series, we can test and verify that the series does, indeed, converge. More appropriately, it converges to one since it’s a geometry series. This doesn’t need explaining.

And finally, a cooler way to think about this (if you ask me). Take 1/9. You get 0.1 where the 1 repeats infinitely many times. Now multiply nine by that. 0.1111111… * 9 = 0.9999999… 0.1 repeating 1 can be represented as 1/9, as I just said like thirty words ago. Multiply that by nine in rational terms and you get 9 * (1/9) = 1.

I think that’s enough said there.
