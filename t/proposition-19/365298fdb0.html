I’m completely lost in this debate between Greeny and mapu. Greeny says that driving high is doable, as long as you’re not too stoned, and mapu says that it affects your reactions. Or am I missing something?

The keyword in Greeny’s opinion is ‘too’. This implies that there is a boundary, after which the effects of drugs do affect your driving abilities, but as long as you don’t cross a certain threshold you are safe. mapu believes this threshold lies between not being high at all, and having inhaled a pinch of smoke. Greeny believes that the threshold differs for each person.

Obviously, Greeny is right. Everyone should know their limits, and act accordingly. Speljohan is right when he says that when you’re driving a car it’s not just your own life, but also that of others. So everyone that feels that he is too high to be able to judge traffic situations, shouldn’t drive because he knows he passed his limit and shouldn’t endanger himself and others.

There’s one question left: Do people actually know their limits, and do they act accordingly? There are a lot of substances that affect your driving abilities, like alcohol, drugs (medicines), perhaps nicotine (are there any studies about this?), and the drugs discussed here. I know that alcohol makes you believe that you can do more then you actually can, so even one bottle of beer can make you a bad driver without even knowing it. Prescription drugs have a lot of negative effects, some make you drowsy, some make you nervous etc.

So how can you prevent substance related accidents from happening? By setting one standard: Don’t use and drive. Because it’s too much of a hassle to judge everyone individually, limits are set that apply to everyone. At the moment, alcohol is barely allowed, but prescription drugs are hardly regulated.

It’s a shame that a lot have to suffer because a few are irresponsible, but if it prevents accidents in exchange for me not being allowed to drive for a few hours I know what to pick.

They can be quite dangerous. A few months ago, a 92 year old man was spectator during the ‘Eleven-city tour for bicycles’ (combine Frisian and Dutch culture and you get a 240km bicycle tour you have to complete in a single day). When he left, in his car, he had to go a little distance in reverse. There still was a crowd about 10-20 meters behind him. He probably did his best trying to leave his parking space. Currently, everyone between 60 and 65 can get a new licence without a medical checkup, and that license is valid until he or she turns 70. After that, you have to get a medical checkup before you get a new license. Thing is, the license you get after you turn 65 is valid for 5 years. I don’t know if you have any experience with it, but old people can deteriorate heavily in 5 years. Usually, relatives will try to prevent old people from driving, but old people can be stubborn too, and have to feel the consequences before they understand that they are a hazard. Too bad people get hurt that way.

Speljohan:

Prescription drugs are strictly regulated here. Altho, Sweden is the strictest country in the world when it comes to substances and traffic.

We have one law that states that:

Dutch law:

It is expressly forbidden to drive or for a driver to drive while he is in under the influence of a substance, which he knows or reasonably should know, that its use - whether or not combined with the use of another substance - may reduce ability to drive, or that he must be considered unable to properly drive

(Translated with Google Translate, so it may sound a bit odd)

The Dutch government even has a website about driving will using prescription drugs. But it all boils down to you being a responsible driver, as there is no way to prove whether you were under the influence of prescription drugs. There is no limit, like there is for alcohol. So it’s illegal, but you can do what you want because they can’t make a case against you.

A while ago there was a documentary about prescription drugs, especially about anti-depressants (altough heavily off-topic, it was on the public tv station. The Dutch public broadcaster actually criticizes the government when they can, commercial stations don’t). What followed was a parliamentary discussion. I have no idea what happened since, but the law hasn’t changed.
