That’s a fair question. Those activities that you’ve listed all involve a continual investment of time throughout the life of employment. It isn’t a one off hour or two of work and a bit of luck.
With that said, I have no qualms with paying for such services.

I suppose what I said above didn’t entirely clarify my view. I don’t agree with the fact that most money comes from return on investment.

Back to the television example. Someone might buy infrastructure to launch a national TV service (like Foxtel).
For argument’s sake, let’s say this infrastructure costs 1 billion dollars up front.
Now let’s say that this TV service costs $40 a month, and has a penetration rate of maybe 10%. In a country like Australia, this is about 2.3 million people.
That’s 92 million dollars monthly, which means that, in just one year, the value of the investment has been entirely recouped.
BUT, the infrastructure is likely to work for a decade or two, with maintenance that is fairly negligible in price compared to the original investment (electricity costs and perhaps damage from storms, whatever).

I know my example is likely an oversimplification, and 1 billion dollars may not be an accurate up front investment that a TV service would expect to pay. But the ideology is the same.
Bottom line, 90% of the country are locked out of a service which in this case is directly available to them (there’s literally radio waves floating over their homes), but denied to them out of sheer spite. And if 100% of people were given the access for say $5 monthly instead of $40, the company would actually be making MORE money anyway.

And that’s it. The same is easily applied to most music – Nine Inch Nails notwithstanding, because Trent Reznor proactively encouraged theft of his music; even the musicians know that the record companies are ripping people off, big time.
Instead of charging a price that would allow virtually 100% uptake of a product, they choose to charge 10% of people a price that is, quite frankly, so inflated that it should make most people sick, and by doing so they lock everyone else out.
