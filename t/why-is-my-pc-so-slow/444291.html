You have described a typical virus that wants to harm the operating system. Most malware these days does its best to not hint you in any way that the system is compromised.

It’s wrong to rely on diagnostic tools in the system you’re inspecting to convince yourself that the system is clean. If the malware is sophisticated enough the functions used to get info about processes/files/registry keys/network connections are most likely hooked - and it filters out the info it doesn’t want you to see. When looking for an indicator of an infection I recommend monitoring connections for your PC in the router.

Anti-viruses are mostly useless. They only protect people who recklessly download files - and they protect you from the mainstream malware only. Any malicious program can be made to bypass an anti-virus when correct encryption techniques are used.

It’s also safe to expect that a ~6 month old OS installion is most likely infected. No matter how careful you are, during those ~6 months a vulnerability in your system will probably have been exploited. That’s why you should perform regular full cleanups on your system, not just formats but full zerofills.

SilentCJ August 3, 2016, 3:09am #17

Easy and most effective fix: back up files and reinstall windows. Also consider buying an ssd for a boot drive, I did this recently.

Long way (edit: actually doesn’t take that long, do most of this on a weekly basis anyway):

  1. Use ccleaner to clean temporary files and other unneeded files. Tick all options. Then use the built in registry scanner.
  2. run > msconfig, go to the boot tab and untick everything that is not absolutely vital to the running of the computer.
  3. Give it a defrag, I personally use defraggler which I developed by the same company as ccleaner.
  4. Don’t have too much ‘registry cleaners’ and definitely not too many anti viruses, it’s just unneeded. When they are all actively monitoring folders for incoming viruses it can really slow you down. Just stick to one and keep it, for me it’s just Microsoft Security Essentials, it does the job and I don’t even know it’s there.

I tend to re install windows like every month anyway, I keep all my files stored on my network drives so it’s never a huge hassle to do so.

Solid State Drive, get used to the term, 5 years time and it’ll be the norm, already is becoming the norm in newer laptops, particularly ultrabooks.

Cons: the cost, and sacrifice of capacity, not a problem with me with server based storage though.
