I am not so sure about the so called “protocol”; whether it can be copyrighted, or not. I think I have even heard somewhere that this “protocol” is something used by alot of different programs just for the sake of efficiency (the algorithm used). I might be wrong, however. Anyhow, I believe the biggest problem would be that these server-sources have in one or another way been involved with reverse-engineering of the client, that is, in fact Jagex’s.

There are one way, though, with which you can get around copyright infringement. But this involves two different teams. The first team must examine the system, and create a specification covering all to be added. When this is done, the specification must be granted by a lawyer, who checks whether it is legitimate, or not (does not use copyrighted material). If granted, the other team (who must not have any connection with the first team) will implement the system according to the specification provided. This method is called “The Clean Room Design”.

Just a side-note:
I don’t really think a totally new game trying to “mirror” RSC would be a great idea, unless it would actually mirror it. By this, I mean, re-making everything would make this a totally new game. When it comes to new games, I think, for it to be successful (as RSC was), you have to come up with something new and original. Today, the most games uses alot better graphics, and this is almost essential. RSC uses such “bad”, but yet nice graphics (nice and/or fun graphics according to my own opinion) because this was a problem back then (when it was created). Not alot of people had high internet-connection speed back then, and this would drastically decrease the servers capabilities of holding “high” amounts of players (if the graphics was better). Also, the server was most likely written in Java, which was not that good and/or fast back then…

I also guess the graphics was that “bad” compared to others, because Andrew was “somewhat” new to 3D-programming… I might be wrong. But that is what I think.

In the United States and many other countries, even if an artifact or process is protected by trade secrets, reverse-engineering the artifact or process is often lawful as long as it is obtained legitimately. Patents, on the other hand, need a public disclosure of an invention, and therefore patented items do not necessarily have to be reverse engineered to be studied. One common motivation of reverse engineers is to determine whether a competitor’s product contains patent infringements or copyright infringements.

Reverse engineering software or hardware systems which is done for the purposes of interoperability (for example, to support undocumented file formats or undocumented hardware peripherals), is mostly believed to be legal, though patent owners often contest this and attempt to stifle any reverse engineering of their products for any reason.
