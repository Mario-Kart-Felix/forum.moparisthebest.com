I’ll start with the two things you have said:

First, the crocodile thing. Evolution does not have a goal, it’s merely a process that favours those born with more favorable characteristics for their environment. Slight genetic mutations could mean a cat could run slightly faster than others of the same species, that is more likely to be able to catch prey faster than others, it would be able to outrun predators better and thus be able to survive for longer, and in turn more likely to reproduce to pass on that gene.

Similarly, a cat of that species is born with a characteristic that hinders it, it means it likely won’t survive as long and perhaps won’t reproduce to pass on that gene. Evolution isn’t about getting to an intelligence like us, a shark is just as evolved as us, and is perhaps far better suited to it’s environment to us, so in some respects evolution has favoured that.

Being like humans is not a goal of evolution. Intelligence suited our ancestors and thus it prevailed over other characteristics, and was continually passed down and improved upon because of how it made is successful in our environment. A crocodile is as much aspiring to be a human as we are aspiring to be a crocodile, in evolutionary terms. A crocodile is very well suited to it’s own environment and is a successful hunter.

However, that is not to say evolution is perfect. We carry vestiges, sometimes negative ones from our evolutionary past that the natural selection process has not got rid of, yet. Our wisdom teeth, for example. We don’t need them, sometimes, if they grow - they can become painful and ruin the straightness of our teeth and thus have a detrimental effect on the effectiveness of a bite. Wisdom teeth are like they are because our ancestors (over the past few million years) had smaller skulls but with larger jaws and used those teeth to break down plants, like we see gorillas do now, they’ll chew plants on those back teeth.

But as our jaws shrunk, as our diets changed, those teeth are no longer useful but haven’t disappeared completely. Not for all of us. However, they are slowly being removed from the gene pool, more and more people are being born without wisdom teeth every year, that is evolution. However small it may seem, that is what evolution is, loads of tiny little changes over millions of years. We don’t randomly give birth to a whole new species every now and again, like some people seem to believe. Thankfully, us, as humans, live in a society that is counter to natural selection. We look after those who are born with disabilities, we don’t dominate the weak as other animals do because of our intelligence and our understanding of each other.

On your other question, why are apes still around if we evolved from apes. That is a misunderstanding. We didn’t evolve from the apes that are around today, we evolved from a common ancestor.

So there was one species of animal millions upon millions of years ago that we all share ancestry with, but different environments, different conditions had us all split off into different species. We are still part of the ape family, and gorillas, chimpanzees, bonobos, etc, they have evolved with us and they are dominant in their environment, but they are, like us, still evolving. Their evolution will be dictated more by natural selection than ours though.

Evolution is a fact, and is accepted as so among the scientific community. Including those who are religious, they perhaps reconcile evolution with their belief in that God started the process. I don’t believe in any God, so I don’t accept that, but I would much rather people accept science and continue their belief than assume science, despite it being humanities greatest tool, you use it’s results every single day, the paper your bible is written on was a result of science, is out to get them. Everything we know as true is through scientific process, even if it seems far away from the advance sciences today.

Look up the DNA evidence, look up the the vestiges we carry due to the evolutionary process, look at the vestiges other animals carry due to that process, look up the fossil record (but, really, the DNA evidence is strong enough that even if we didn’t have such a strong fossil record, it wouldn’t mater), look around you!

What do we do with dogs? We breed them in select conditions with select partners to get the best characteristics for a breed. That is evolution. It isn’t evolution through natural selection, but it demonstrates the process rather well.
