Death_Style August 4, 2016, 5:51pm #25

I had an assignment to fix my professor’s broken math expression calculator that involved recursively digging into user-input parenthetical statements and holding values on recursive function calls down the stack and back up. in any ordinary language it would be trivial to figure out what you were looking at but with the godawful syntax (((((((()()))(()))()()))))) and names (seriously, set!, car, cdr?) I spent way too many hours just trying to figure out what abomination I was even looking at before I even began coding the solution.

it’s not bad for small functions but trying to make something actually productive and readable in that language is a fallacy to me. you have to use a new set of parenthesis just to declare variables and the deeper you go the harder it becomes for another person to even grasp what is happening. I also think having to iterate a list just to reach an element in question instead of just directly accessing it is mindblowingly inefficient when you think about every list an application would use. granted you probably shouldn’t be using a list if you need to directly access certain members, but consider how horribly inefficient a list/merge sort would be. you can’t even do a binary search with that kind of limitation. I’m sure the code looks pretty but that doesn’t mean much when it’s marginally slower. maybe it’s just my imagination and the implementation isn’t so bad, but it’s definitely not something I’d want to do in scheme.

I’m probably biased since the day I first read a line of clojurescript but w.e

http://inst.eecs.berkeley.edu/~cs61a/su10/resources/sp11-Jordy/vector-vs-list.html

but yeah, sometimes writing imperative code will be far more practical. that’s why most lisps are multi-paradigmal, so you can do what you actually need to do. in reality you can’t be completely pure without sacrificing something, especially when you are dealing with states that are constantly changing (like i/o). i mean haskell has a bunch of solutions to that, but meh, it’s fuken haskell. fp is just an ideal; if something can be done well without modifying state, then nice.

most editors in general are not suitable for editing lisp code. coloring parens and correct spacing make things much more readable.

car and cdr are historical names, and brevity was important back then.

fp emphasizes functions, where each function should be relatively small with one specific purpose. of course this can’t always be done perfectly but in general if you split up your functions things will be easier to comprehend.
