Difficulty is purely subjective, changing your operating system to GNU/Linux shouldn’t be a problem provided that your hardware is supported (which it most likely will be even if you have to rely on third party repositories) most GNU/Linux distributions today ship with a graphical installer which will automate the process for you.

Linux itself is just a kernel and requires the usage of other utilities this is where the GNU project comes in; in the Linux world there exists what is called distributors, these people (generally large groups of people) distribute the kernel along with utilities from the GNU project and other modifications to make a working product. (They’re called linux distributions)

I won’t make any personal recommendations as you havn’t specified as to what you actually want from the operating system; the important thing to remember is that Linux distributions are all Linux (in the sense they all ship the linux kernel and that really is the most fundamental part) Distributions generally only tend to differ in terms of package management and release models (as well as a few other small things such as kernel version)

On the subject of viruses unfortunately all software is prone to errors and security exploits, regarding security on GNU/Linux in general it is known to be highly secure due to its implementation and market share, in other words you can get viruses but you’re extremely unlikely to do so (I havn’t had a virus and i’ve been running GNU/Linux for 2 years) you won’t need an antivirus scanner but it may be useful if you plan to transfer files over to Windows.
