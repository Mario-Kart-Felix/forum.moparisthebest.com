Who vs. Whom; A lesson on grammar

General Discussion
VB7_Rocks July 30, 2016, 10:19pm #1

Well apparently not so many of you are aware of when to use who vs whom in a sentence.

Who: Subject
Whom: Indirect / Receiver / Observer

For example, if you’re talking about someone performing an action, use who. If you’re talking about someone receiving or being an observer to an action, use whom.

Examples:

“Whom are you talking to?” “To whom are you talking?” – You are doing the talking, and someone else is receiving the action of talking.
“Who is there?” – Someone is there, performing the action of being.
“Whom did you give it to?” “To whom did you give it?” – You are doing the giving, someone else is the receiver of the action.
“To whom it may concern” – It is doing the concerning.
“Who gave the gift to you?” – Someone else was doing the giving.
“Who killed him?” – Someone was doing the killing.
“The people who abused him were put in jail.” – Him is the receiver of the action of abusing, which was performed by someone else.
