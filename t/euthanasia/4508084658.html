I think a problem worth mentioning is the emotions such a patient may be feeling. They may not be able to rationally make the decision to end their own life. Some may consider this valid, considering ending the pain is the same regardless of how the decision is made. I feel that even in cases of terminal illnesses, people would still avoid allowing themselves to just die if given the ability to rationalize it. There are most likely ways to measure neurotransmitters and hormones to figure out if someone is thinking more or less rationally, but I’m not sure if that would hold up in court.

Speaking of which though, if such a concept were ever to be implemented into society, it should definitely go through the court system. It’s not like a judge should be the decision maker on someone’s life, but they kind of are already, for severe criminal trials. I think the family should have a major say as well, just not the deciding factor. The kind of stress and pain a family has to go through to reach that decision is unbelievable. To do so shows a lot of what they believe and that they care enough to give the person up just to ease a few months of pain.

Assisted suicide, also known as euthanasia, is a hotly-debated topic recently. Whilst a lot of people believe that every human being has a right to life and therefore has the right to end their life, the right to life differs in that decisions can not always be turned back on. The right to freedom of speech differs from the right to life in that if you choose to give up your right to speak by remaining silent for an hour, you will be able to speak again whenever you like. With suicide, however, decisions cannot be changed if the suicide is executed completely.
