sinisoul August 2, 2016, 3:50pm #11

Didn’t do much client rewriting today since I was sick but started to look over and work on the networking piece. To be truthful I always wondered why servers could never hold two thousand players. That’s the goal of a good base, right? But I have never seen a server hold more than 500 that is a modern server. Given Runesource is a great base to give example of but hearing lately that the maximum amount it has held is about 500 is shocking. Where did people go wrong was my first question and then I started to think about the history of why the fuck people use NIO. If none of you remember it was all the rage about a year ago from this April. I’ve been investigating what design patterns I would enjoy to use for networking and I have found that IO would simply be easier. I don’t know why people have always vouched that NIO is always better because from reading articles and receiving advice from a good friend of mine, I don’t see a large margin of difference. Well, the margin of difference that people tell me about NIO being better. It’s all a bunch of hooey. If people had any understanding of how IO functioned in a whole then we would have better servers. I mean that too. For sciences sake, the packets are set up in a way where you don’t need to rape the read method to grab the available bytes all the damn time.

So with that in mind I have started to write a little networking implementation to create an update server by hopefully the end of next week. Thanks to those of you who support this project.

So youve decided to use IO? Rather than do that you should look into blocking NIO.

Personally from a threading perspective in NIO I see a few options.

  1. Single threaded reactor design. This design is the basis of runesource.
  2. Multithreaded event based reactor. Basically what Netty, mina, and other libraries are at their core.
  3. Hybrid less orthodox approaches. Lately I’ve been toying with some networking ideas.
    A) Using multiple selectors, one per thread. This would follow the traditional reactor pattern but keys would be assigned to a selector on a certain thread which would handle them for their life.
    B) Simple multithreaded reactor. Somewhat different because unlike other networking libs, the design follows a clear cut way of asynchronously executing networking tasks. This is what I ended up doing for my current project. I call it my no bullshit networking api.
    C) Blocking Nio.
