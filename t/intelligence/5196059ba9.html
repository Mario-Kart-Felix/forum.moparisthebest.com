Fire capez:

Huh? If you’re implying that a savant would think I was wrong I would like to see what evidence we have to suggest that natural ability really does exist; if we define natural ability as that which is inherited in terms of physical composition i’m more inclined to agree with you but in respect to cognition unless i’m mistaken there’s no ‘empirical’ proof that intelligence is inherited, and I don’t see how it could be really.

Why would ‘natural ability’ have to be inherited?

I mentioned savants because they generally have what could be considered ‘natural ability’ that wouldn’t necessarily translate from other skills as you seem to imply. Musical savants may not pick up an instrument and be able to play Mozart instantly, and they may need years of training, but musical savants can pick up an instrument and learn to play it proficiently in just a few short years, and most importantly they don’t need formal training. Musical savants also usually have extremely good memory, capable of playing any song they have heard before nearly perfectly, and many musical savants can also play a song nearly perfectly after only hearing it once.

There’s also artistic savants and more notably mathematician savants, who can memorize entire phone books, who can calculate numbers in their heads up to 39 digits, who can do complex math faster than a calculator and sometimes instantly. Sometimes these abilities have to be worked on, but they’re not a case of ‘oh, this person can learn a skill quickly’, millions of young children grow up with musical instruments and millions of children grow up with musicians for parents, millions of young children are taught instruments from a young age. The difference between a savant or a child prodigy to that of a professional adult, is that a savant will be as proficient in a skill as a young child on a similar level to an adult who has been practising that skill their whole life.

Also, as a point of interest, autistic savants usually have a range of conditions ranging from learning difficulties to blindness and deafness, blind musical savants with learning difficulties are particularly common(among savants, that is). In some cases, savant abilities can diminish as the savant ‘learns’ other abilities. There was a case of a young girl who possessed remarkable artistic abilities from an extremely young age, on the level of people like Leonardo da Vinci, but then lost their artistic ability as they learned to talk (or for other reasons, it’s a single case and the reason wasn’t particularly clear).

It’s not like musical savants with learning difficulties can apply their cognitive thinking skills, or mathematical skills, to music, especially when they can play on a professional level before they can speak.
