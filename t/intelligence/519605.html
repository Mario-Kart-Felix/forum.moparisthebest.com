Fire_capez August 4, 2016, 7:26am #5

There have been entire books written on the subject of intelligence, many people claim to know what it is and use it in their daily lives and yet there isn’t actually an accepted definition (you can look at a dictionary all you like but it’s a bit more abstract then that). To me what makes someone intelligent is their cognitive functioning, if they posses high mental ability (e.g can do tasks quick, think outside the box) then they’re intelligent, if they can remember a few facts such as who the 21st president of the United States is that doesn’t make them intelligent, it makes them knowledgeable. So to me what separates intelligence from knowledge is that intelligence is the application of knowledge in order solve or form a complex idea (e.g a scientist who knows what a gene is isn’t intelligent but a man who then goes on to make a theory that allows genetic engineering is quite frankly, very intelligent)

It’s also worth noting the idea of relative intelligence, that people are only intelligent at certain times. Everyone is capable of being stupid as is everyone capable of being smart at some point in their lives it’s just the average and consistency that really determines whether or not someone calls you intelligent.

davidi2 August 4, 2016, 7:28am #14

Here’s how I’ve always viewed it:

Knowledge is how much ‘information’ you know. Facts, etc. Information you can recall from memory. Has nothing to do with how you got that information or how easy/hard it was for you to learn it.
Wisdom is based on experience, and is how you view the world and different situations. Wisdom is not something you can be taught.
Intelligence is a few things. It is how easily you can learn new things, how well your memory works, analytical power, etc. You can become more intelligent in some ways, but not in others. This is why some people need to study for hours to understand something, and others don’t need to study at all. Intelligence is the most complex of the three for sure.
