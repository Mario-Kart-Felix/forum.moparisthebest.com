zstars July 31, 2016, 10:46am #15

It depends on your specific needs. However, generally you will want to store references to the entities on a hash map, sorted by their ID, so that ID queries can be done efficiently and with O(1) complexity. As frank said, you will also want to use some sort of scene graph. There are several alternatives, and depending on the game and other factors some may be more efficient than others. Some popular ones are: BSP trees, quadtrees, kd-trees, octrees, spheretrees, speedtrees. Most trees will allow logarithmic spatial queries in the average case ( O(log(n)) ). Depending again on the circumstances, sometimes two different structures are used, one optimized for static objects and another for dynamic objects (For example, a PR-tree for static and a point auto-balanced quadtree for dynamic).

Not sure who you are replying to as I don’t think anybody made any emphasis on accessor methods or suggested any particularly stupid solution, but assuming you are using that loop to look for a requested id, as you seem to be doing, it is not a good approach from an algorithmic perspective.

A brute force algorithm such as that is O(n) and in the case of the 30000 entities would need to perform by average 15000 operations before it finds the right player. Also, that approach assumes IDs are perfectly sequential at any given moment of time, which is often incovenient.

On the other side, a binary tree (O(logn)) would take by average 15 operations (versus 15000) to find the player.

A hash map (O(1)) would take by average a single operation to find it.

Note that this would also apply for spatial queries. Obtaining players-in-range lists for every player through a brute force algorithm would be O(n^2) whereas with an structure such as a quadtree it would be O(n logn).

For 30000 entities, this is 900,000,000 operations versus 446,181.
