I don’t agree with this. I wouldn’t say I’m from a poor background, but more I don’t ask for financial support from my parents. I’m given a £400 allowance each term from my dad, which usually goes toward drink/food.

I’m paying £3,290 a year for my tuition fee, and then £3500 a year for my student accomodation. This is all paid for through the student finance system, and by the end of my 3 year degree, I should be £23,000 in debt. Now, assuming that tuition fees do rise to £6,000 a year tuition, absolutely anyone, poor or rich, will be able to finance this through a student loan which, under the new government guidelines, will be provided extremely generously. Not only this, but depending on which course you apply for, student can recieve grants, which don’t have to be paid back. For example, this year alone, my Computer Science course will have given me £1200 and the government will have given me £2906 in grants.

I think the way we pay for higher education here is very good, and I’m not against the rise in tuition fees at all. A rise should surely mean a higher quality of education, a more generous way to pay for the debt associated with higher education, and surely more would be attracted to higher education because of the generousity of the loan.

Debt is something that most people will have to take in order to reiece degrees, masters and PhDs here, and I think the debt is totally worth it. I completely agree with Thug Life’s post about being provided with an opportunity to further your career as a result of uni.
