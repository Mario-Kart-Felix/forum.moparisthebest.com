CodeForFame August 1, 2016, 12:33pm #17

[quote author=CodeForFame link=topic=496947.msg3634410#msg3634410 date=1308501581]Maybe whoever is doing it recognizes that they will get more over time if they keep hacking middle-class players. If they hack the rich players or mods, you’ll be looking a hell of a lot harder for the mistake; possibly to a point where you start shutting down services or removing features to prevent admins/mods from getting hacked.

I’m happy with how passwords are encrypted on RSC. I monitor every query and when someone gets hacked I check to see when their password was changed and 9 out of 10 times the ‘hacker’ logs in using their original password. That means that they know the non-encrypted password and haven’t changed it. Even if they got access to the database or SQL injected the site they would only get the hash (which is a mixture of encryptions and salted). Plus, as I said it’s very rare that a big time, sensible player gets hacked… I’m convinced it’s passwords from leaked databases, bots or simply brute force.[/quote]

There are multiple things that could be going on here.

There could be a bug where they can authenticate without actually knowing the password, with something equivalent of the famous ’ or ‘1’='1 SQLi query.

The passwords could be being hijacked from the server side when they enter their password on the website somewhere.

It could be a brute force attack (like you suggested), or using passwords from another DB to get a decent idea of where to start guessing.

I mean with just those two things there is a ton of code to go over to make sure that none of that is happening, and it’s by no means an easy task, because it can be extremely difficult to identify some security flaws.

On the client side of things, there is a huge amount of things that can be happening…

They could be selling their account, be keylogged, be victim of a man in the middle attack, have their password in an old DB, be an idiot, etc.

Anyway, it comes to a point where the user needs to be responsible for their own security, and the service provider (you) needs to do an integrity check of the system to make sure there wasn’t a security breach. It’s a pain in the ass, but if you want people to feel secure, you need to make sure they know exactly what you’re doing, and how much of an effort you’re putting forth to ensure it wasn’t a security issue on your end, especially when they already have massive doubts.
