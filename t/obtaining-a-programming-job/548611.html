zyle1992 August 4, 2016, 2:07pm #8

I’ve worked got Xerox, Amazon, Microsoft, and Nordstrom. All you really have to do is know your stuff for the job you’re applying for. Generally, phone screens ask you the basic questions. If it’s an SDE role, it’s mostly C#/Java questions, if it’s an SDET role, there will be testing questions as well (what unit testing is, test driven development, what to test given a certain situation, etc). How to implement certain algorithms, like Stack, Linked List, cyclical linked lists, how to implement a certain function iteratively or recursively (ie. calculating a specific fibonacci number), what the difference is, why you would choose one over the other. If you don’t know something, be honest. “How would you implement an ecommerce website with Angular?”, if you don’t know the technology, state it. Employers hate nothing more than believing you know a technology, and then finding out you’re incompetent when they ask you to do something.

Once you get in, you’ll likely be working in an Agile/Scrum lifecycle, so knowing what the basic process is would help. But most of my experience is in enterprise systems. I haven’t had a lot of experience in little 1-2 person dev shops.

Vix August 4, 2016, 2:07pm #9

The need for programmers is in rather high demand right now since many companies are currently looking for websites and applications. I’ve done internships with a few local software development companies who really stressed that a large part of obtaining the job is how well you work and interact with others since almost all projects are done in small groups and managed by a single individual. The general stereotype of a programmer is a socially awkward person who doesn’t interact well with others, most companies won’t hire someone like this because they worry that person could slow down development because of an inability to communicate. Education is very important too don’t think i’m hinting that it isn’t, but ten degrees still wont seal the deal if you cant make eye contact with your interviewer.

If your sporting a 2+ year degree from either a college or trade school, work well in groups, and are enthusiastic (or at-least pretend to be) about the job your applying for you should be alright.
