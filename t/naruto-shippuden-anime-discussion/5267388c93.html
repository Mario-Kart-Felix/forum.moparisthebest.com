It’s acceptable to identify with characters and attempt to imitate them, but it’s not normal to completely mimic a character. I often desire to be like characters who are calm, rational, friendly, and selfless. I think about these traits for a few hours or days, but afterward, I disregard them and move on; change occurs gradually, and one must put effort into it. I think the person you’re studying mimics fictional characters because he is not happy with his identity. He would rather assume the identity of someone who is intelligent and respected, loved, etc. If he is your friend, and if you care for him, you should explain the situation to him and attempt to help him recover. Explain he is using anime (the different story universes) as escapism, refusing to acknowledge and deal with his troubles. Find out why he is depressed, actively listen to his reasons, try to understand his position/state of mind, be there for him and be patient, and hang out with him/try to get him to become more sociable (don’t be pushy). If that doesn’t help, read books on psychology, become knowledgeable about related or relevant subject material (depression), and/or seek help from friends/family or professionals.

There’s nothing wrong with being motivated by a fictional character. The character Dexter’s job as a blood splatter analyst is interesting, and I once considered majoring in forensic science. I’ve heard Neon Genesis Evangelion helps people recover from depression. Also, Welcome to the NHK provides commentary on hikikomori (socially withdrawn people); the anime is basically about a girl trying to make a guy sociable again. You can use these two series to help that individual.

It’s not right to criticize anime for the guy’s condition. He could have become this way after reading books, watching movies, or by doing nothing. It’s his own fault–whether intentional or not–not anime’s. So, no, anime doesn’t make most people depressed. I always say, “There’s always a choice.” If you are unhappy with something, you often are able to do something about it. If the guy wants to get better, he has to put in the effort. You can’t just expect life to get better. If he wants to stay depressed, that’s the choice he made.

“In any situation, no matter how confining, you have a choice. To believe you do not, is to choose not to choose.”
