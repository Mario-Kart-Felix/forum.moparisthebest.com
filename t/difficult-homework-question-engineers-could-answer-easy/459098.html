The question.

A first class lever in static equilibrium has a 50lb resistance force and 15lb effort force. The levers effort force is located 4ft from the fulcrum.

What is the Actual mechanical advantage (AMA) of the system?

Using static equilibrium calculations, calculate the length from the fulcrum to the resistance force.

I find this difficult to do in 9th grade.

ExtremeX-Scape August 4, 2016, 12:01am #2

Static just means that it is not moving. A first class lever is one that has the fulcrum in the middle between the resistance force and the effort force (thank you Wikipedia). The reason that it is not moving is that the moment (force times distance) generated by the effort side is the same as the moment generated on the resistance side. Effort times effort distance = Resistance times resistance distance. They tell you that the effort force is 15 lb and the effort distance is 4 ft so the effort moment is 60 ft-lb , and we know that the resistance moment is the same (because it is in static equilibrium. So the resistance distance (length from the resistance force to the fulcrum) is 60 ft-lb / 50 lb = 1.20 ft.

Let me get my engineering class stuff out from last year. Iirc moment is when the ft•Lbs are equal. Like on a teeter totter.

First thing you need to do is calculate the distance between the resistance force and the fulcrum. You can figure this out by figuring out the moment for static equilibrium(sorry if my vocab is bad, its been a while).
15lb • 4ft = 50lb • xft
60ft • lb = 50x ft•lb
60ft•Lb/50lb = x ft
1.2 ft = x ft.

Now that we have the distances, we need the AMA. AMA is calculated by the ratio between the resistance force and the effort force.
50/15 = 10/3 = 10 : 3.
