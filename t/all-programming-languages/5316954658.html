I think we’re talking about different things.

If you were to jump in at the shallow end with no prior knowledge, pick up a nice high level scripting language, then with a few weeks of work you would probably be reasonable competent and able to solve problems and write half-decent code in your language of choice. There’s nothing wrong with this, and it doesn’t make you a bad person or whatever. Most of us probably started out this way, and it’s not a a terrible way to go about it. All I’m saying is that it isn’t necessarily the best way.

But at some point, you’re going to come up against a problem you can’t solve in your chosen language, or maybe you just want to try a different language. Without a fundamental knowledge of how things work at a low level, you’re going to struggle to pick up or understand new concepts as easily as someone who already grasps the underlying structure of the computer.

My crappy analogy would be learning a new language. Let’s say you know English, and you want to learn Spanish or French. You can pick up a phrase book, and start out that way, soon enough you’ll have enough of an understanding to manage a conversation with a native speaker, but it’s not going to make you a competent linguist by any means. On the other hand, if you want to truly understand languages (for this contrived analogy let’s just assume we’re talking about European languages, most of which are derived from Latin), you’ll want to pick up something low level that will give you a better understanding of how languages work, structure and semantics and all that. With a firm grasp of the basics, you’ll find it a lot easier to pick up a new language, but more importantly you’ll be a competent linguist.

So, the TL;DR of my spiel is: if you don’t know the basics, you’ve no chance of being a truly ‘good’ developer.

As for whether or not C is the best language to learn first, I don’t have any stupid analogies left to make my case, so maybe it’s just a personal preference thing. I certainly wish I had made the effort to learn it when I was just starting out…

xx matty xx:

C is a shit starting language. It might help you understand how programs actually run, but there are so many caveats to catch you out. Learning programming is hard enough as it is without having to worry about shit like memory management.

C is one of the easiest languages to pick up and master. Syntax and the standard library is predictable and minimalistic. C has also been around nearly forever in the world on modern computing, thus it can be found almost everywhere. This being said, there are many books and online guides/resources available to those who are interested in learning C. Another implication is C’s maturity: the language has very clearly definied standards that are solidified and standardized within the public domain. If your platform doesn’t have a C compiler, something is VERY wrong.

Caveats? Other than the potential annoyance of endianess I really cannot think of any true caveats. Of course, there are many solutions to the aforementioned including endian.h (http://man7.org/linux/man-pages/man3/endian.3.html). You mention manual memory management as a big downfall, but I and many seasoned C programmers see it as a huge advantage you usually cannot obtain in a higher-level language. Furthermore, the ‘caveat’ of memory management isn’t much of an issue if you are familiar with the codebase you’re working with: just be careful, follow patterns and debug often (a principle that applies to all languages). Did I mention gdb or valgrind yet, or any of the other tools out there, including static analysers? There are plenty of libraries and tools out there to provide a ‘modern’ experience when programming in C.

Finally, I find it funny you jumped on the anti-C bandwagon, but you didn’t mention C++ (which was mentioned in this thread). If anything, C++ is one of the most inconsistent, caveat-filled complex language ever conceived.
