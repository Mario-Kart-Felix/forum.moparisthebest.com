I’m in CS at my university and looking at the upper-year course syllabus’ you will learn a lot of the theory and mathematics behind CS not just applied programming. I’m sure this applies to most if not all other universities since CS isn’t just about programming…

CS will give you a breadth and depth of programming and computation that probably covers anything there is to know about it (theory and application-wise). But it’s up to you, you’re basically choosing a tradeoff between learning theory and enhancing your programming knowledge in general, or sticking to one particular domain such as game programming.

You must not know any game programmers. Game programming is part of the CS degree field. Pretty common to switch between the different fields.

@OP: it’s not as simple as going into CS and “specializing in game development.” That’s silly. Game development is an entire industry of development in itself, with a wide variety of job paths. Some parts are extremely difficult if you’re into more cutting edge solutions (this also applies to other fields of CS that big tech companies tend to have to solve), otherwise there’s tools programming in game development which is super similar to general CS. There’s also gameplay programming which is more of what I consider true “game programming,” but that’s neither my interest nor my forte, so I’m not going to discuss it much.

Just take various courses and see what interests you. There are schools and graduate programs that specialize in certain tracks, including game development, AI, graphics programming, networking, systems engineering, etc. The field is absolutely monolithic. Just look around and take a class that’s interesting.
