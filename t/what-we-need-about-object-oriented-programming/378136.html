To me, private servers are dying. Slowly, people are becoming far more dependent on “Help” sections rather than learning on their own, which on a larger scale leads to a slower advancement of private servers in general. Over the past few years, servers have been taken on a skyrocket. Four years ago a server with magic spells was epic, a server with a mini-game was the best. As time progressed, the community learned more as a whole. People had to rely on themselves, as most others were on the same “level” of experience as they were, and from this came more intelligent beings. While servers progressed, one had ‘less’ to do, as servers were becoming more complete (or more fulfilling) as RuneScape, and with the addition of constant help (truly spoon-feeding the answers) the community has been drawn downward in advancement. More and more users are downloading servers with a lot of content, and with so, the ‘owners’ or ‘administration’ of the servers don’t even need to go as far as adding a tutorial. How could one learn without making any modifications?

Development needs to increase. Jagex laughs at us. Private servers are all the “same” now, which is why most of the experienced developers are quitting. Constant battles as to “who’s better” take place daily, which only causes more of a slow-down to RSPS advancement. If people are going to develop private servers, they need to understand OOP (object-oriented-programming), which can’t be truly “taught” to one. People should understand the basic concepts of OOP at the age of 10. Everything in an environment (the Earth) is an object of some sort, whether it be flowers, trees, people, cities, atoms, WHATEVER! Many things in our world can contain another. A school can contain classes, which can contain students, and so forth. Not only does our world have object-containing objects, however TONS of objects have sub-objects. A student is a sub-object of a citizen, which may be a sub-object of a human, which may be a sub-object of a mob (mobile entity), which is a sub-object of an entity (anything that exists). It all relates to real-world, which is why programming in OOP is extremely simple yet powerful as well. In the real-world, we have blueprints for lots of stuff, and when speaking in OOP terms, these are called interfaces. There are a number of situations in software engineering when it is important for disparate groups of programmers to agree to a “contract” that spells out how their software interacts. Each group should be able to write their code without any knowledge of how the other group’s code is written. Generally speaking, interfaces are such contracts. For example, imagine a futuristic society where computer-controlled robotic cars transport passengers through city streets without a human operator. Automobile manufacturers write software (Java, of course) that operates the automobile—stop, start, accelerate, turn left, and so forth. Another industrial group, electronic guidance instrument manufacturers, make computer systems that receive GPS (Global Positioning System) position data and wireless transmission of traffic conditions and use that information to drive the car. The auto manufacturers must publish an industry-standard interface that spells out in detail what methods can be invoked to make the car move (any car, from any manufacturer). The guidance manufacturers can then write software that invokes the methods described in the interface to command the car. Neither industrial group needs to know how the other group’s software is implemented. In fact, each group considers its software highly proprietary and reserves the right to modify it at any time, as long as it continues to adhere to the published interface. As we know, everything in our world also have attributes. As an example, a flower some attributes like color, name, size, petals, location, and ect. Just like the real-world, this can be done in OOP. Every attribute is a “type” with certain “modifiers” (conditions) of who has access to it, and ect. Let’s start out with the most easy-to-relate-to “type” (data type) called an “integer”. An “integer”, as we know from math, is any positive or negative whole number. So as an example, a flower has an attribute petals which is set to 5, meaning the flower has 5 petals.
