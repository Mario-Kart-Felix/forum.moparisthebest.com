when talking about general server design, NIO is a term that annoys me as it refers to Java’s NIO API; the correct term would be asynchronous IO. ask a purely C and C++ programmer about NIO and he’ll have probably never heard of the term before, let asynchronous IO roll off of your tongue and he’ll baffle on endlessly. sorry for my nitpicking, but it has to be said.

also, in an ideal world, i’d recommend not educating him as to what thread-per-client even is, it’s a blasphemous and hideous practice that makes anyone with a brain look down upon the user. it’s a shame it’s pretty much the de-facto standard for teaching people how to handle multiple clients in a server, and so is probably unavoidable.

OP, i’m assuming you want to use Java for this and so the logical first step is to gain a good grasp of the language itself. then, research into basic sockets and communication and learn a little bit about how it all works internally. try to understand everything, and also allow yourself to realise the flaws of thread-per-client on your own; thinking about something yourself is always the best route to a partial revelation. look at the Java NIO API and asynchronicity as a whole as well as looking at patterns like the reactor and the proactor which the NIO API hands itself well to. then i’d strongly advise on looking at design patterns in general, a place where the Gang of Four book becomes a complete godsend. rinse and repeat your standard and practice and ultimately realise that your dream is not as far off as it may seem, although… if you want to make a functional first person shooter you’re also going to have to learn a lot about graphics and game development as a whole, i’d recommend just sticking with being a back-end developer.

and may i iterate that this is probably going to take a long time, concurrency and networking is not a topic that you can become very familiar with in a matter of hours.

_s pretty much hit it dead-on.

In order to figure out how to write any program, you need to break it down into the problems that it needs to solve.

What does a server need to do?

[ul][]Accept incoming connections
[]Handle the I/O of each client
[*]etc.[/ul]

This approach is brought forth with the concept that all computers, and computer programs, exist to solve problems. Once you have broken down your software into the problems that it needs to solve, you start planning out a design (the most important thing you can do) that solves all of these problems in the simplest and most efficient way possible. The greatest thing that separates amateur programmers from expert programmers is the ability to plan out a design and execute it. Some people can design things all in their head, others like to use pencil and paper, others like to use modeling languages like UML.

I learned in college classes that you need to plan everything out before you even write one line of code. This is especially important in collaborative projects. From my personal experience, things always work out better if you plan ahead. You don’t see construction crews just slapping down a multi-million-dollar bridge without planning anything - they use blueprints, they plan every inch out meticulously. Software engineering isn’t all that different from architectural engineering.

Weeks of coding can save you hours of planning!

in addition to this, a good tip for the future is to solve each problem separately. a modular and decoupled architecture is seldom a bad one and each module should abide by the Unix philosophy of doing one thing and doing it well (Eric Raymond’s design rules are something i abide to religiously). also, with modularity comes the inevitable possibility of code reuse. look at libraries like netty for Java and libev & libevent for C/C++, they’re very flexible and were created with adaptability and modularity in mind as well as being applicable in nearly every project where IO is a factor to consider in design. look at the way they strive to achieve the goal, use them as an example and write all of your modules to be applicable in nearly every context, just like they are. blake said that software engineering isn’t all that different from architectural engineering, i can only reiterate upon his notion to a greater extremity. software engineering barely differs from architectural engineering, you plan and build, the only differences are the concepts used in planning and the tools and materials used to build.

tl;dr - realise one thing: software engineering is an art.

[Back on topic]

Let’s put it this way, I’m guessing your an adolescent thinking game development is pretty darn cool (which it is), however, coding in general is becoming ever so advanced, and the concepts of game development are being driven by world leading industries in game development (e.g. SONY) that strive for perfection, so it’s unlikely that you will be making the new “perfect” Call Of Duty game.

Now, don’t get me wrong, all game developers started from somewhere, but not without the fundamental basics that are needed to succeed in making a decent game.

Perhaps your wondering what the hell I’m talking about? Well, in order to make a successful game (in your case, an FPS) than you will need most of these things:

Knowledge:

  • The basic knowledge of general coding; the most popular gaming code languages are C++, and JAVA, so I’d start learning these thoroughly before attempting to draw guns on GIMP.
  • Understanding servers and clients.
  • Understanding how IDE’s save time and efficiently construct the base of your game.

Team’s:
Have you considered the effort needed to develop a game, no? A major company such as Infinity Ward usually takes 8-14 months to successfully develop a game (e.g. Modern Warfare 2). Now let me tell you, Infinity Ward is not run by one person, it is a company of several teams working day-by-day consistently. Imagine trying to accomplish what 50 people can do by yourself. That would take you a VERY long time. So you will need to come up with a team, which then brings me to my next topic.

Cost:
You will need to provide for your development team, this means an office, multiple computers; hundreds of thousands of dollars will be needed in the development stages of your game.

I hope I have enlightened your mind of what game development consists of; not just cool animations or graphics, but the determination to create something amazing. Please, i hope i haven’t effected on your dreams in the future, but keep in mind that game development is only attempted by the determined, not script kiddies who know how to make themselves spawn items on a Runescape private server.
