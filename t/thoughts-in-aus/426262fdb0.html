The main crash types that an inexperienced driver will be involved in are ones that actually involve running off the road and being T-boned. Head on collisions and rear end collisions also occur, but to a lesser extent.
For more experienced drivers, the rate of head on collisions and running off the road decreases (because of increased driving aptitude) while rear end collisions increase (because of increased driver confidence).

So to answer your question from my point of view:

  1. Speed cameras are worth putting in locations where there is a demonstrated need for them. This is the policy at the moment, on the record at least. I’m dubious though because there are plenty of speed cameras on highways where doing an extra 20km/h would not be a problem for the vast majority of drivers. In my opinion they’re largely ineffectual unless drivers aren’t made aware of their existence (by law, signs must appear on the approach to a speed camera in NSW).

  2. Speed bumps are great in lower socioeconomic areas where there is a demonstrated need for them. For example, someone decides to drive along my street at 100km/h or more each week, despite the fact that it’s a residential area with a 50km/h speed limit, cars parked on the side of the road, and potentially pedestrians (although this is unlikely at midnight, unless you consider me coming home after a party).

  3. The education here in Australia is largely hands off. We have television advertisements, but no driver education in PDHPE at high school. I regard the advertisements as highly ineffectual because most people our age are desensitised to that kind of thing. Unless you’re prepared to have a teenager attend an accident in person, you’re not going to really have an effect on them.

While society is risk adverse, the individual is not. If 8 times out of 10 are good odds for living then why wouldn’t you be a little reckless sometimes? That’s where the legislation falls over.
The government’s biggest problem is that it can’t save everybody. No politician will ever say that though.

One of the reasons that I see for problem driving amongst younger people (like getting together in groups and acting like hoons) is a complete lack of anything entertaining to do where I live.
There just isn’t that much to do when you’re under 18. Actually, there isn’t that much to do in Newcastle even after you reach adulthood, so it’s a wonder that there isn’t a huge market for hallucinogens and similar.

t4:

How strict are they with the logs (assuming you don’t wave them for $40 after 2 years)?

In Queensland you get your learners permit at 16, and you have to complete 100 log book hours, some of which must be with a driving instructor which are worth 3 hours for every 1 hour, and 10 hours night driving. If you complete those hours in the first year you can go for your P1 license at 17. Victoria is much the same just you can go for your P1 at 18 after doing 120 hours and you have to take a hazards test. In Victoria you get your permit for 10 years, in Queensland a learners permit is 3 years and a P1 is an optional up to 5 years.
You can also apply for an exemption of years too in Queensland, and wave them the $40 earlier
