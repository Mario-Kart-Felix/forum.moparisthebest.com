The Holocaust was the state-sponsored, systematic persecution and annihilation of European Jewry by Nazi Germany and its collaborators between 1933 and 1945. (Source: United States Holocaust Memorial Museum) Jews were the primary targets, but many others were targeted based upon their race, ethnicity, handicap, or nation of birth. In addition, Hitler and the Nazi Party targeted Jehovah’s Witnesses, homosexuals, Gypsies, socialists, the handicapped and the mentally ill.

This poem refers to the bystanders who did nothing to help others during this time of mass chaos. Many people were scared, alone, and fearful that they were going to be targeted next. The Holocaust took place from 1933-1945, and much of what we know about this horrible time came from the men, women, and children who survived this catastrophe. Survivors are still living today, but will not always be with us. If you have the opportunity to meet a survivor, do that. For more information, please visit the Holocaust Survivors website.

Why it happened

Hitler came to power because the people of Germany were desperate for change. Hitler convinced them that the Jews were the ones responsible for Germany’s economic and political problems. Many Germans went against their values and beliefs out of desperation and for what they believed to be a greater cause. Bystanders including individuals, groups, and nations made choices not to act against the Nazi, anti-Semitic leader. Those who spoke out were silenced. Many feared Hitler and thus he grew powerful; he was a force that was not easily defeated.

Kristallnacht

November 9–10, 1938, was Kristallnacht (translated: “Night of Broken Glass”); the “beginning” of the Holocaust when Nazis broke the glass windows of synagogues, and ransacked Jewish-owned stores and homes. These rioters burned buildings and killed 91 Jewish people. This night sparked the fire that led to the anti-Jewish policy that would wind up becoming the Holocaust. For more information on the “Night of Broken Glass”, please view the Kristallnacht museum exhibit.

Ghettos

As the Nazi’s began to force Jews from their homes, ghettos began to grow. The Germans set up ghettos (sections of the city designated for Jews to live) in Nazi-occupied Europe as a first step toward eliminating the Jews. The largest ghetto was the Warsaw Ghetto where over 400,000 confined Jews occupied an area of 1.3 square miles.

Concentration Camps

The ghettos were temporary housing. From 1933–1945 as a means of imprisoning millions of victims, Nazis established forced labor camps, transit camps, and extermination camps. Built exclusively for mass murder, the extermination or concentration camps confined people to life under very harsh circumstances. Forced into railroad freight cars and exposed to inhumane conditions, prisoners traveled to their destination. Many of those who made it alive to the camps, surviving disease, hunger, lack of sanitation, and dehydration were led into “showers” which were gas chambers in disguise. Nazis forced others to work for them until the Allies liberated the camps in 1945.

How the Holocaust Ended

The Holocaust ended when the Allied forces defeated Germany and liberated the camps beginning in 1945. Hitler killed himself on April 30, 1945, which led to Germany’s surrender on May 8, 1945.

Results of the Holocaust

In the aftermath of the Holocaust, survivors lived in DP (Displaced Person) camps, as many no longer had a family to whom they could return. The camps, run by the Allied powers, were open until 1957. During that time, many Jews emigrated to Israel, other European nations, and to the United States. The crimes of the Holocaust devastated the Jewish population and Jewish communities.

For more information regarding the Holocaust, please visit the United States Holocaust Memorial Museum.

For more information about the Auschwitz Concentration Camps, please visit Virtual Reality Movies: Auschwitz.
