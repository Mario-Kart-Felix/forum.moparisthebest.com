I understand that in a very simple P2P network model, Peer 1 asks the central server for a peers IP, let’s say Peer 2. Peer 1 then takes Peer 2’s IP and starts sending and receiving data from them. The two peers can now communicate without the central server, and neither of them can listen on server ports because they don’t have port forwarding set up.

I’m struggling to understand the actual method of how Peer 1 communicates with Peer 2 when neither can listen on a server port.

It’d make sense if Peer 2 sent the data back to the central server, then Peer 1 could retrieve the data from the central server - but that defeats the object of a P2P network.

Cheese_Police July 31, 2016, 4:01pm #3

Most commonly P2P communication is done with UDP. UDP doesn’t require a persistent connection and clients can choose to receive data from whoever they want whenever they want. It is still filterable, and some routers may filter/block UDP traffic, but in most cases that is not the default. UPnP is also a possibility you might want to look in to.
