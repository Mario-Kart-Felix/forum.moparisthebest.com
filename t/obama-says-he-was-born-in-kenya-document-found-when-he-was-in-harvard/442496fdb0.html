  1. In New Zealand, the milk goes straight from the cow to the tank via the milk line and milk pump, there are no “containers” in between.
  2. If a cow is sick, it’s milk is milked straight into a bucket and disposed of. The cow is given a round of anti-biotics and is only allowed back into the milking herd after a set time, ie 4 days.
  3. The quality of the milk is tested every day as it is picked up, the farmer receives a receipt which states milk quantity, Somatic Cell Count (The cow produces more of these when she is sick or when she is old, it can make the milk taste different), milk fat content, milk solid content, protein content, bacteria test results, sediment test results and other items.
  4. The bulk milk tank must be washed twice with hot water and acid before any milk is added to it. After every milking, 20-45 minutes is spent washing the entire shed with either acid or alkaline solution (alkaline is every 4th day). The wash water must be at least 80 degrees celcius by the time it has gone through the system.

It is not guaranteed to be safe, but there is only a very very little chance of it being unsafe.
If the SCC rating of the milk exceeds a certain value, the milk company stops picking it up straight away and the farmer gets a large fine. If any penicillin is found in the milk, the farmer gets a huge fine. If the temperature of the milk is above 7 degrees celcius at the time of pick up, the farmer gets a huge fine. If there is too much water in the milk, the farmer gets a huge fine. Any issues with the farm are resolved quick smart.

My uncle’s cows have the second highest quality milk in all of NZ, and probably quite high up there in relation to the rest of the world. The milk from my dad’s cows is not quite as high quality but it has never gotten anyone sick, so meh…
