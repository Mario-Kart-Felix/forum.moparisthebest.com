Thanks!![/quote]You can definitely teach yourself everything you could learn at university, and I’m sure a lot of interviewers would be more impressed if you turned up with a solid portfolio of experience rather than a university certificate. The problem, at least in the UK, is that most companies look for graduates and you wont get past HR without a 2.1 degree. I think you’d have to work a lot harder to find a decent paying job if you went down the self-taught route. CS graduates can pretty much walk straight out of uni and into a graduate scheme or permanent position.

You could of course take a different approach and go into freelancing or found a startup. Then nobody is going to care about your degree, but obviously higher risk etc.

Ultimately I think they’re all valid choices but it depends which one suits you personally.

I’m still confused on how applying for an internship interfered with your studies. Maybe it’s a different process in my area?? Here we apply for internships in the fall/early spring and do them over the summer.

I mean, you should be trying to do internships AND get passing grades…but like I said, this is going to vary greatly from area to area and company to company on how much one or the other matters.

Point is, learn and get yourself out there, you’ll get a job. Internships will help pay for school and also provide an experience to talk about if you do manage to snag an interview later.

If you were an employer, which would you pick?

  1. You interview a student with an admirable GPA. He/she has no experience to talk about other than school projects, thus no experience on large-scale projects.
  2. You interview a student who has mostly As/Bs, and Cs, and during the interview talks about past projects he/she worked on for previous companies.

Employers in my experience so far are much more interested in what I have worked on and couldn’t give two shits about how I did in my Calculus III class. Grades come into a larger factor when you’re applying for a large company with many applicants, because it’s an easy weed-out.
