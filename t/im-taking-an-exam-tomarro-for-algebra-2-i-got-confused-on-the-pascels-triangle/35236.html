Pascal’s triangle is simply a “geometric” form of the binomial theorem…so if you know that, you can solve any problem like this. Tbh, I don’t know a way to continue Pascal’s triangle without using the binomial theorem (I’m sure there’s a way tho).

Since we don’t know the form for a power of 6, we can use the general binomial theorem formula (which can be seen here as I have no clue how I’d write it here: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_theorem)

So for our 6 iterations, we get the results:
0: y[sup]6[/sup]
1: -12xy[sup]5[/sup]
2: 60x[sup]2[/sup]y[sup]4[/sup]
3: -160x[sup]3[/sup]y[sup]3[/sup]
4: 240x[sup]4[/sup]y[sup]2[/sup]
5: -192x[sup]5[/sup]y
6: 64x[sup]6[/sup]

You can add em together and get the answer Hope that’s right, lol. I don’t know if you covered this though, so if you know how to work out the next line of pascal’s triangle, you can just use those coefficients. You have to remember, though, to consider the coefficient for x in this case before working it out.

Well, if you can work this out more easily with Pascal’s triangle, lets first start by taking the relevant line:
(a + b)[sup]6[/sup] 1 6 15 20 15 6 1

Basically, that means:
(a + b)[sup]6[/sup] = 1a[sup]6[/sup]b[sup]0[/sup] + 6a[sup]5[/sup]b[sup]1[/sup] + 15a[sup]4[/sup]b[sup]2[/sup] + 20a[sup]3[/sup]b[sup]3[/sup] + 15a[sup]2[/sup]b[sup]4[/sup] + 6a[sup]1[/sup]b[sup]5[/sup] + 1a[sup]0[/sup]b[sup]6[/sup]

Note that taking anything to the 0th power returns 1 (with the exception of 0, but we’ll leave that for now). So, simplified:

(a + b)[sup]6[/sup] = a[sup]6[/sup] + 6a[sup]5[/sup]b + 15a[sup]4[/sup]b[sup]2[/sup] + 20a[sup]3[/sup]b[sup]3[/sup] + 15a[sup]2[/sup]b[sup]4[/sup] + 6ab[sup]5[/sup] + b[sup]6[/sup]

Because a = 2x and b = -1y, you also have to apply the appropriate exponent to the coefficient. So, just by plugging everything in (and remembering that when y had an odd exponent the entire term becomes negative), you get the answer as I have above
