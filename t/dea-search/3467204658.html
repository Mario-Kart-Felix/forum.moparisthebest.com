yakman July 31, 2016, 5:34pm #24

I found a dusty old book the other day, it contains a very interesting quote that I think is relevant to this discussion about Britain and America.

The year is 1803.

In Paris, the US minster to France, Robert Livingston, was also frantically trying to acquire land in Louisiana, specifically near the vital gulf coast port of New Orleans. Louisiana, retroceded to France by the Spanish court in 1800, was a truly vast territory, which posed a serious thread to US aspirations. Worse, its great seaport amid the swamps of the Mississippi delta controlled the passage of some 40 percent of America's exports. The United States desperatly needed land or, better still, the entire city of New Orleans. But Paris was showing relcutance to do business. Just when the situation seemed hopeless, Livingston, through an admirable sleight of hand, won - simply by putting it about that the United States was considering a rapprochement with Great Britain. Such an idea was exactly what France feared - a reunited Anglo-Saxon empire. This gross misrepresentation by the Americans induced Napoleon Bonaparte to react favourably towards the US request; instead of granting its wishes, the emperor sold the United States his entire Louisiana territory, all 828000 square miles of it, for a mere 60 million francs, or $27,267,622. Overnight, the land area of the United States more then doubled. But Napoleon was no fool; apart from the useful cash, "the sale assures forever the power of the United States, and I have given England a rival who, sooner of later, will humble her pride."

Wikipedia helpfully provides a map of the Louisiana purchase as the event became known.
