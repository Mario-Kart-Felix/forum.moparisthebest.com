Richard Wright, the author of the novella, [u]Rite Of Passage[/u], develops Johnny Gibbs effectively through characterization to form this immensely complex and three-dimensional persona. When Johnny goes to the movies, the reader can easily relate to his situation, as the average reader enjoys watching a intense movie. Another example is a time when Johnny attained good grades, a rush of accomplishment that every student can empathize with. These events truly strengthen Johnny Gibbs as a character. Johnny is a crisply dressed fifteen year old with good grades. The reader can infer that his cultured upbringing through his supportive parents and encouraging siblings had a profound effect on his growth. Through their positive influence, he naturally becomes the teenager that he is. This facet of his life brings Johnny to life from a societal and appearance perspective. In light of his stellar school work, it is not surprising that he is well-versed in the English language. Again, those around him influence him in a positive manner. His family and friends speak exquisitely, allowing him to speak in a keen manner. Because of this language, Wright truly allows the reader to see Johnny as a multifaceted character. Another notable aspect of Johnny is his poise and composure. Grace under pressure is a valuable asset, and Johnny is extremely graceful under pressure. As he fights Baldy, he is able to draw from his reserves of courage and strength in the tussle. In answering the police officer’s questions, Johnny is still reserved, keeping his cool. This allows the reader to truly embrace Johnny as a sympathetic, complex character.
