You probably don’t even need this by now, but oh well.

First we have to look at how the partial pressure for a gas would increase. Pretending it’s ideal we have:
P=nRT/V
meaning that P and n are directly related by RT/V. So if we increase the number of moles of carbon monoxide, we also increase its partial pressure.

Solid’s have constant concentration based off of their density, so their concentration will never change. As a result they are not involved in the shifting of the equilibrium. As a result there is only one mole of equilibrium-shifting reactant per two moles of equilibrium-shifting products.

A) would have no effect on the system
B) would favor the side with the greatest number of moles, the product side
C) would favor the side with least number of moles, the reactant side
D) would favor reactant side
E) would favor reactant side

So the answer would be B

Let me explain this more clearly. The higher the pressure, the more the equilibrium shifts to the mole-light side, so the inverse must also be true. Imagine that a reaction always wants to go to the mole-heavy side, and that the equilibrium only begins to shift to the mole-light side once pressure increases. Although that is a completely contrived example, it does allow one to rationalize what happens.
