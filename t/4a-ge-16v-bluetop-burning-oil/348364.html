Don’t know if this is your case but here’s what I found

(http://www.newcarbuyingguide.com/index.php/news/main/840/event=view)
1. Bad valve seals
2. Worn valve guides
3. Pressurized crankcase (oil pan) due to a clogged PCV valve or breather system
4. Blow-by from worn piston rings

And surprisingly yahoo answers… (http://answers.yahoo.com/question/index?qid=20091230135616AAVQRqF)

Best Answer - Chosen by Voters Cam, rod or main bearings have absolutely no "bearing" on why your motor uses oil. If the bearings were bad the crankshaft would be laying in your driveway or a connecting rod would be a new part sticking through the side of your block.

If the motor has no visible external leaks, the only other possibilities are worn piston rings, plugged up oil rings, worn cylinder bores or worn valve guides. The areas I’ve listed allow excess crankcase oil to enter the combustion chamber to be burned.

Causes are directly related to a forgetful oil change maintenance schedule or an engine with a thermostat stuck open. What kind of oil do you use? What viscosity is it? How often do you have it changed? Does your heater kick out high temperature heat once the motor has warmed up?
Source(s):
Alignment, suspension and brake shop.

When a car burns oil it will put out BLUE smoke, WHITE smoke means you are burning water or anti-freeze. A little is alright when you start it because it is generally just some condensation, but if your water level dropped and steady white smoke is coming out only one thing is possible. You have a blown head gasket.
