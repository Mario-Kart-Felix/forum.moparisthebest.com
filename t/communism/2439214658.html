If the parents, or in high school the students, hear that a teacher isn’t doing his job right, they tell the representative of their counsil.

Let’s say that Mr. Jones is a teacher. He tells his students that they can do whatever they want, as long as they don’t tell anyone. Mr. Jones get’s paid, but he doesn’t teach his students anything. As you saw in one of my previous posts, there is a middle or city counsil. That counsil deals with things like education per school. If needed, some people are asked to come and tell about the teacher. If the counsil thinks that it’s running out of hand, they plan a meeting. They invite the teacher, students that he teaches, parents and if needed other teachers. In this case it’s obvious that the teacher gets fired, and he’ll probably wont be a teacher anymore.

[quote=“bowserjr2, post:34, topic:243921”]Who has the more power, the theacher or the headmaster?

The teachers decide what is best for their school, they don’t need a headmaster. The city counsil tells them what the parents expect from the school, and the teachers plan how to do that. That’s plain logic, because not everyone knows how to run a school. Lets say that in school A they teach Economics, English, Maths and Spanish. Teachers that teach Spanish, don’t know how much about Maths. So it’s obvious that Spanish teachers compose the booklist for Spanish, and the Maths teachers compose the booklist for Maths. So the lower level of running a school is dealt with.

But to run a school you also have to decide what blackboards you need, how many computers you need etc. This is done by all teachers. They come together and explain how many, and why, they need an x amount of computers for their course. Then they make a plan on how many they actually buy.
