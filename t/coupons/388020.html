Coupons?

Yes, the 78 cents was basically tax. Let’s say an item was $1.50 and I had a coupon for 75 cents off. My store doubles coupons, so I receive the item for free. In other cases, there’s a “Buy $20 worth of X products and get a $10 checkout coupon off your next shopping trip.” The $20 that needs to be spent is always before coupons are used and are usually based on shelf (non-sale) prices, so if I use a $3 off coupon on a detergent that is $5 sale price, but $8.50 shelf price, $8.50 would count towards the $20 rather than the $2 I actually paid for it. When I reach the $20 mark, I usually end up paying $5, which I pay using a previous $5 off coupon from last week and receive a $10 checkout coupon for next week or anytime before the expiry date. Thus, I pay nothing out of pocket and “earn” $5. There’s also many times where I pay nothing and get $x in checkout coupons making the deal a x-dollar moneymaker. Sounds too good to be true, but it is.
