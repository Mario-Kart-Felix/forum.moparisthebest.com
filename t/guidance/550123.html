justaguy August 4, 2016, 2:54pm #3

Being in high school and thinking about your career path is very important and it’s good you’re doing it early. With that said you have several things to consider…

Figure out what field of computers you want to study for your degree. Since you mention CS-related subjects, here are a few off the top of my head:

[ul][li]Computer Engineering - Deals mainly with the hardware aspects of computers such as digital circuits, electronics, computer architecture, programming, etc. You will be taking physics and lots of applied and discrete mathematics (especially math courses that will complement your physics courses) and also programming courses.[/li]
[li]Software Engineering - Deals with the software development life-cycle and programming from an engineering perspective (design, testing, and modelling software). Lots of programming and applied mathematics[/li]
[li]Computer Science - Deals with the mathematical basis of computers and programming; to sum it up generally you’re basically studying algorithms (these include programs). You’ll end up taking a lot of discrete mathematics, and if you want to study theoretical computer science, you will also be studying a lot of logic graph theory. This is what I’m currently taking as a second-year university student so feel free to ask me questions about this either here or via PM.[/li][/ul]

This is generally how my school approaches these. Any creditable program will require you to take atleast a few math courses so prepare yourself early.

My advice for for extending your programming knowledge would be to stay competent with the web (the internet of things is becoming even more relevant these days) and to learn more C and Python. C is great because it’s a statically-typed language that can help you understand computers and lower-level programming while being a high-level language. Python is great because it is a dynamically-typed language and also has object-oriented capabilities which are also relevant. Usually introductory college courses on programming use Python or Java (from what I’ve noticed).

It never hurts trying out different languages, even if you don’t master them, because you learn many techniques and paradigms which are mostly transferable to other languages.

Fat_Cookies August 4, 2016, 2:54pm #4

Knowing the basic syntax of a programming language is useful but if you want to go into further education understanding a programming language is what counts.

Extending on what justaguy said: if you want to go into software development it’s great to expose yourself to as many languages and different technologies! If you like javascript node.js is a great thing to get familiar with. If you like hardware, robotics go learn the in and outs of C and even some assembly - you could pick up a raspberry pi and program ARM assembly!

As a computer scientist I’d recommend dipping your toes into functional programming - python has a few functional features pushed into it on the side however if you really want to learn programming in a functional style you should have a go at learning Scheme. Once you’ve familiarised yourself with Scheme you might want to check out more pure function languages like Haskell or OCaml.

On a side note if you’re still unsure on what you want to study, a bog standard Computer Science degree is a good compromise and will mean you get to study things from all sorts of fields.

I’m currently on my 4th semester with my school’s software engineering program, which is pretty much a combined CS and SE degree at my university, with application classes using hardware as well. So, in my case, I get a mixture of all three majors.

I am currently in Calculus II, and have yet to take Calc III, Discrete Mathematics, Stats, and Differential Equations or Linear Algebra. I am also required to take two years of physics. I am sure it varies depending on schools, but if you’re looking into any form of engineering…buckle down on math, and literally everything else. It (and any form of engineering OR computer-related field) is a challenging program. And yes, Computer Science still has plenty of math. Take any AP math courses your high school offers, because even if you don’t test out of them then you have the experience.

Currently I’ve taken classes utilizing the languages Java, C++, and right now, x86 Assembly. I will learn more languages in future courses.

Look into internships even as a freshman. You likely won’t get one that soon, but I managed to snag one as a sophomore where I will likely be doing some software analysis and working on a small project for a loan-service company. Looks good on a resume, teaches you what the real world is like, and might even help pay for school.
