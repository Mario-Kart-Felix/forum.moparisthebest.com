_s11 August 2, 2016, 4:52pm #3

a request of proficiency with a language doesn’t mean that you need to fully understand the language’s fundamental workings; it’s more of a request for working experience and a broad scope of knowledge and application with said language ie. networking and concurrency. although fundamental knowledge and implementation understanding is useful to know, i wouldn’t say that it’s a prerequisite since most software engineering jobs stray away from working at that level, so while a content developer would probably never need to know anything too low-level, it would be expected of a core engine developer to have knowledge of lower-level topics.

it depends really on what you want to end up doing, but experience is one of the most important things. a self-taught hobbyist with lots of projects, lots of varied presented works and experience working on popular (open-source) projects will have a very good chance of getting a job in comparison to a by-the-book graduate with no real experience, future plans or portfolio.
