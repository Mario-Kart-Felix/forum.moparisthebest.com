Dizzy:

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pure_competition

Pure Competition is best for the consumer, and worst for the companies. As you noted, it’s very easy to buy supplies. That’s not a good thing.

Perfect competition requires that the following six conditions be fulfilled. In such a market, prices would normally move instantaneously to economic equilibrium.

Atomicity
An atomic market is one in which there are a large number of small producers and consumers on a given market, each so small that its actions have no significant impact on others. Firms are price takers, meaning that the market sets the price that they must choose.

Homogeneity
Goods and services are perfect substitutes; that is, there is no product differentiation. (All firms sell an identical product)
Perfect and complete information
All firms and consumers know the prices set by all firms (see perfect information and complete information).

Equal access
All firms have access to production technologies, and resources are perfectly mobile.

Free entry
Any firm may enter or exit the market as it wishes (see barriers to entry).

Individual buyers and sellers act independently
The market is such that there is no scope for groups of buyers and/or sellers to come together with a view to changing the market price (collusion and cartels are not possible under this market structure)

These are the basic characteristics of perfect competition. As you can see, the Grand Exchange makes this approach perfect competition. We’ll use yew logs as an example here.

No single person has a large share of the market, and thus, individuals cannot influence the price. Maybe someone sells 10k yew logs all at once, but there are probably millions of yew logs being traded at any one time.

Each product is identical. A yew log is a yew log. No brand recognition.

Perfect and complete information. RS sets the price. They tell you the market price which you should buy at.

Equal access. Anyone can get to 60 wc and cut yews at basically any place. No transportation costs.

Free entry. If you have 60 wc, you can cut yews whenever you want.

Individual buyers and sellers act independently. There is no central price setter except for the Grand Exchange itself. No trusts can be formed.

In pure competition, price of an item equals the marginal cost. Each item is sold at the lowest people will reasonably sell. There will always be yew macroers not wanting to wait to sell, so they just offer it at a lower price than the market price to get the cash as fast as they can. A constant input of these yew macroers will drag down the market price down significantly.
