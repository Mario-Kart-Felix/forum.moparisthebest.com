Eh…that’s questionable. I’ll start with your last question first.

Obviously, matter can neither be created or destroyed, which is what I think you’re referring to. But relativity states a relationship between mass and energy (E = mc^2). Obviously, this works on the very small scale, but it has been shown in particle accelerators that if two particles collide at near the speed of light, they will somehow transform into a high energy photon (destroying mass, in a sense) and then revert into some particle and anti-particle (creating mass, in a sense). So, although it is theorized that the universe was absolute nothingness before the big bang, there was still “the big bang” singularity, which is supposed to be huge amounts of energy packed into a “point” of “infinte density and zero volume.” So, considering E=mc^2, the matter in the universe wasn’t just created from nothing (and on an off topic point, apparently all the matter in atoms we can see in our universe makes up only 4% of of the universal composition).

Why hasn’t be big bang happened again? Since the initial one we know about, there hasn’t been another big bang singularity we know about to actually cause one. There is an oscillating universe theory in which after the big bang, the size and density of the universe reaches a critical point and reverses itself into the “big crunch”…but apparently, its been “proven” that the universe is flat (in regards to the warping of time and space) and will not do that…
