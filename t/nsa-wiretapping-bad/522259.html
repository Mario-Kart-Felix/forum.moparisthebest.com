General Discussion
xEthanx August 4, 2016, 8:53am #1

Topic
As many of you may know, the NSA for the past years has undergone warrant-less wiretapping of American citizens in attempts to achieve the goal of reducing terrorist threats. The NSA had been doing this for years without telling American citizens. Well, then how did we find out about it?

The answer is Edward Snowden. Snowden is a non-anonymous leak that had been working for the NSA under a cushy salary for some time. I’m not going to try to explain what he says, because I can just show you:
[youtube]https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5yB3n9fu-rM[/youtube]

Now what I want to ask you people is simple: which side do you take? Recent opinion polls - if to be believed - say that 65% of Americans are now very skeptical of the NSA and feel the right to some answers. I guess what I’m asking is (if you’re an American), do you feel that the NSA should have listened in to the millions of everyday conversations through technology without telling us or without obtaining a warrant first?

Thesis
I have several contentions on this. Firstly, I am going to argue, that whether or not you feel the NSA should be doing this, this at least should’ve been a decision made by the citizens. Or, at the very least, debated publicly by politicians before it happened. Barack Obama and George Bush should have, at the very least, let the American people know before it was done, or better yet, allow some sort of democratic strategy decide on it.

Second, the act of warrant-less wiretapping directly contradicts the 4th amendment of the Constitution, which states:

“The right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures, shall not be violated, and no Warrants shall issue, but upon probable cause, supported by Oath or affirmation, and particularly describing the place to be searched, and the persons or things to be seized.”

The American people have the right to their privacy if the NSA never received a warrant. Now, this doesn’t necessarily mean their hiding something - many Americans everyday will tag what locations they are on Facebook and tell people constantly what they’re doing on Twitter. Many peoples’ lives are out in the open. But there’s a noticeable difference here. Through social media, it’s voluntary. No one was forced to account for every little detail of their life. Being a participant of the NSA wiretapping was in no way optional.

My final contention is that Barack Obama really has just given up on civil rights this time. In 2007, he spoke out adamantly against wiretapping under the Bush administration, and vowed that he would dismiss all of it. Now, in 2013, he is making petty excuses like “well, if you wanna be safe, you’ll have to lose a few rights.” That is a valid contention to start a debate Mr. Obama, but it directly contradicts the 4th amendment to the Constitution. You are allowed to express disagreements with the Constitution, but until these amendments change, you must follow all of the amendments strictly.

Those are my views. What do you all think?
