Adding to what jme said:

Maturity develops in the adolescence stage (identity vs. role confusion), according to Erik Erikson. This is after the Formal Operational stage of cognitive development (according to Jean Piaget), which provides abstract reasoning and potential for mature reasoning.

For those of you who associate maturity with morality, the above can occur around the stages of conventional to postconventional (according to Lawrence Kohlberg). However, everyone develops morality different, and thus stands by their morals in unique ways. Not everyone reaches the stage of postconventional, but one would assume many do.

For those who wish to associate maturity with human needs, I would guess that maturity really starts to grow during the self-esteem and self-actualization stages (highest 2 stages of the Human Needs Hierarchy by Abraham Maslow). Of course, not everyone reaches self-actualization, but anyone who lives past adolescence years develops self-esteem (which relates it to the aforesaid on adolescence).

For those who wish to tie it in strongly with cognitive development, the Frontal Lobe finishes development just after adolescence. For those who are unfamiliar, the Frontal Lobe is responsible for conscious reasoning and decision making, or cognition. As the wikipedia article states, In humans, the frontal lobe reaches full maturity around only after the 20s[1], marking the cognitive maturity associated with adulthood.

All of the above is associated with psychology or neurology (last one). You could believe in one particular bit of evidence above, believe none of it, or believe all of it. I personally would use all of the above to describe maturity, as I did here. I find that our humanistic descriptions of our emotional and conscious states are being driven by multiple psychological and neurological factors simultaneously, all of which are impacted both by our experiences and heredity.

I apologize for slightly going off topic, but I hope my opinions/evidence on the topic are clear enough.
