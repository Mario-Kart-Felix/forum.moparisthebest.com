I confess. When I first started reading this chat log, I thought it might be better suited to someone with more immediate problems. I began thinking of those other people, then of my children and finally I took a better look at myself. Sanga828 uses clear exposition and dramatized case studies (as I recall a persuasive approach employed by Sigmund Freud himself). But the author is of the cognitive school that rationally espouses: 1) identifying what formed a problematic attitude, 2) noting how it has affected you, 3) working through your anger, sadness and grief, 4) and, as an adult, now engaging in a healthier mode of behavior.

It was about a third of the way through that I realized Niall was applying a bit of psychology to me, its reader. The author examines rape mechanisms in a very threatening way and then asks us to do some self-assessment now that we were aware of the methods we use for denial. How could anyone who up to this point has agreed to his common sense approach decline? The result—utilizing worksheets on eleven “life areas” and an extensive array of positive and negative characteristics of self-image—is a useful plan of action identifying problems, visions, short-, medium- and long-term goals. I found this incredibly helpful.
